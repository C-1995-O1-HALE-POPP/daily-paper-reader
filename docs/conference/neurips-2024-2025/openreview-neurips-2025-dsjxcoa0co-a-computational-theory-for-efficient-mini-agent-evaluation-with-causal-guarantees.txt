## Abstract 

In order to reduce the cost of experimental evaluation for agents, we introduce 1

a computational theory of evaluation for mini agents: build evaluation model to 2

accelerate the evaluation procedures. We prove upper bounds of generalized error 3

and generalized causal effect error of given evaluation models for infinite agents. 4

We also prove efficiency, and consistency to estimated causal effect from deployed 5

agents to evaluation metric by prediction. To learn evaluation models, we propose 6

a meta-learner to handle heterogeneous agents space problem. Comparing with 7

existed evaluation approaches, our (conditional) evaluation model reduced 24.1% to 8

99.0% evaluation errors across 12 scenes, including individual medicine, scientific 9

simulation, social experiment, business activity, and quantum trade. The evaluation 10 

time is reduced 3 to 7 order of magnitude per subject comparing with experiments 11 

or simulations. 12 

## 1 Introduction 13 

Throughout the history of computer science, manually handing the data has been replaced by 14 

computational approach, such as computational linguistic (OpenAI et al. [2024], DeepSeek-AI et al. 15 

[2025]), computational biology (Abramson et al. [2024]), and computational learning (Valiant [1984]). 16 

Here we extend the computational theory to the ubiquitous domain of evaluation. 17 

Randomized controlled trials (Fisher [1974], Box et al. [2005]) are widely regarded as the gold 18 

standard for evaluating the efficacy of interventions, such as drugs and therapies, due to their 19 

ability to minimize bias and establish causal relationships between interventions and outcomes. By 20 

randomly assigning participants to intervention group or control group, RCTs effectively balance 21 

both observed and unobserved confounding variables, thereby mitigating the influence of hidden 22 

common causes (Reichenbach [1999]). However, conducting RCTs to assess every potential agent 23 

is often prohibitively expensive and, in certain cases, unfeasible as shown in appendix A. This 24 

challenge is particularly pronounced in fields like artificial intelligences, where models may possess 25 

hundreds of parameters, resulting in an infinite and high-dimensional space of potential interventions. 26 

Evaluating such agents through randomized experiments is resource-intensive, demanding substantial 27 

investments of time, finances, personnel, and materials (Speich et al. [2018]). Moreover, the extended 28 

duration of these experiments hampers the rapid iteration and optimization of agents, rendering 29 

the process inefficient. Given these limitations, alternative methodologies that balance rigorous 30 

evaluation with practical feasibility are essential, especially in rapidly evolving domains requiring 31 

swift assessment and deployment of new agents. 32 

An improvement is to build the centralized A/B test platform, which has been proven successes 33 

in application updates and recommendation in Byte Dance (Byte-Dance). The implementation 34 

of centralized A/B testing platforms can significantly reduce the costs associated with conducting 35 

randomized trials for researchers. However, this cost reduction does not inherently enhance the utility 36

Notably, the lower cost and increased accessibility of such platforms can lead to a substantial rise in 38 

the number of randomized trials conducted. This proliferation of experiments raises ethical concerns 39 

regarding participant exposure to potential harm during the randomization process. For instance, 40 

as highlighted by Zhou et al. [2024b], the automation and scalability of A/B testing frameworks, 41 

while beneficial for rapid experimentation, necessitate careful consideration of ethical implications 42 

to safeguard participant well-being. Polonioli et al. [2023] emphasizes the need for ethical and 43 

responsible experimentation to protect users and society. 44 

In addition to other agent evaluation works in appendix B, evaluatology (Zhan et al. [2024]) emerges 45 

as an independent filed in recent years. However, there is still a lack of a theory to help us find an 46 

effective and efficient evaluation system that satisfies the concerns and interests of the stakeholders. 47 

Here, we introduce a computational theory to evaluate the effect of given agents. The main benefit 48

33.2 Hidden common cause 104 

The hidden common cause between subject and metric may introduce the confounding bias if the 105 

evaluation task is causality sensitive, such as individual medicine. Causal inference is often used to 106 

handle hidden common cause from data. However, applying existed methods to handle unmeasured 107 

hidden common cause problem in evaluation faced those challenges: 108 

• Hidden identical common cause (HIC) . If the assignment of S is determined by an unlisted 109 

identical common cause E which can be denoted as S := E, and there exists effect from 110 

E to M . We may mistakenly attributing the effect on M of E to S. The identical hidden 111 

confounder can never be excluded from statistical analysis due to the identical property if 112 

S was never controlled by randomization. Therefore, effect from S to M is unidentifiable 113 

in all kinds of existed non-intervention based causal analysis, including diagram-based 114 

identification (Pearl [1995], Tian and Pearl [2002], Shpitser and Pearl [2006], Bareinboim 115 

and Pearl [2012], YAN [2025]), neural-based identification (Xia et al. [2021]), statistical-116 

based identification (Jaber et al. [2019]), observation study (ROSENBAUM and RUBIN 117 

[1983]), causal representation learning (Schölkopf et al. [2021]), and causal discovery 118 

(Spirtes and Glymour [1991], Zhang and Hyvärinen [2009]). 119 

• High-dimensional evaluation subject . Potential outcomes (Splawa-Neyman et al. [1990], 120 

Holland [1986], Imbens and Rubin [2015]) often assumes each evaluation subject must be 121 

sampled once which often do not established for high-dimensional evaluation subjects. For 122 

instance, parameters of AI model can be hundreds or thousands and more complex which 123 

structure is different from continuous dose-level analysis (Schwab et al. [2020], Nie et al. 124 

[2021], Zhu et al. [2024], Nagalapatti et al. [2024]) with low dimension. 125 

For causality sensitive task, any hidden common cause between subject and metric is excluded 126 

including HIC if the subject was randomly, independently, identically sampled or assigned as shown 127 

in theorem 2 and theorem 4. For high-dimensional evaluation subject, the causal effect of evaluation 128 

error is bounded and evaluation result can be generalized to the whole subject space whatever how 129 

large the space size as shown in theorem 4. 130

## 4 Learning evaluation models 167 

In evaluation processes, two critical factors are evaluation error and evaluation cost. Our objective is 168 

to reduce evaluation cost while maintaining an acceptable level of generalized evaluation (causal) 169 

error through computational evaluation. To achieve this, we can minimize empirical evaluation error 170 

because upper bound size is fixed given number of error measurements n and confidence requirement 171 

1 − σ in theorem 1 and theorem 4. 172

## 5 Assessing assumptions 191 

The IRIS assumption can be satisfied in data collection while the other assumptions may not be 192 

satisfied in real world. While the IIDE assumption could be trivially met by employing a random 193 

number generator as the outputs for IID outcome, this approach is devoid of practical utility, as it 194 

fails to reflect any substantive patterns or domain-specific knowledge required for realistic evaluation 195 

modeling. In order to assess the IIDE and unbiased assumptions, statistical analyses can be employed 196 

to verify necessary conditions under those assumptions, providing credibility to the theorems derived 197 

from this assumption for someone. It is important to note that these assessments do not conclusively 198 

prove the assumptions. Instead, they serve as methods to potentially refute the assumptions under 199 

specific conditions, rather than confirming its validity. 200 

5.1 Assessing IIDE assumption 201 

In highly controlled environments, such as CPU evaluation, the assumption can be rigorously satisfied 202 

by independently and randomly selecting tested chips and configurations from an identical distribution. 203 

However, in many open environments, the IIDE assumption is not directly testable on real-world data, 204 

such as individual medicine, and social experiment. 205 

First, the normalized means of error subsets should follow a common Gaussian distribution, as 206 

implied by the central limit theorem. To test this, we randomly generate 30 subsets and apply the 207 

D’Agostino-Pearson test to assess whether their normalized means adhere to a single Gaussian 208 

distribution. Second, error distributions across different splits should be identical. We randomly 209 

partition the errors into 30 subsets and use the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test to determine whether the 210 

distributions across these subsets are statistically indistinguishable. 211 

5.2 Assessing Unbiased assumption 212 

Our effect error bounds requires unbiased evaluation models while sometime the evaluation model 213 

may not be unbiased. To assess the unbiasedness of the evaluation model, we employ the following 214 

statistical procedures: 1) Global Unbiasedness Assessment: We conduct a one-sample t-test on the 215 

entire dataset to determine if the model’s errors have a mean of zero, indicating unbiasedness across 216 

the universal set; 2) Subset Unbiasedness Assessment: We randomly select 30 subsets from the dataset 217 

and perform one-sample t-tests on each to evaluate whether the mean errors within these subsets 218 

deviate significantly from zero. This step examines the consistency of the model’s unbiasedness 219 

across different data partitions. 220

6.1 Evaluation model 227 

To demonstrate the generalizability of our evaluation model in agent space, we consider 11 distinct 228 

scenes. In these scenes, computational evaluation can be helpful to reduce experimental cost, shorten 229 

simulation time, or enhance the efficiency of advertising and insurance sales. 230 

Although subjects are independently, randomly, and identically sampled from the subject space, they 231 

cannot be randomly deployed in real-world settings for direct measurement of benefit-related metrics 232 

(such as mortality, rehabilitation time) due to experimental limitation. Instead, our evaluation system 233 

defines the evaluation metric as the post-experiment metric on unseen test data with randomized 234 

6treatment. The metrics capture the causal effect from subject to post-experiment outcome, as 235 

established in Theorem 4. Empirical studies (Ai et al. [2022], Zhou et al. [2023], Zhou et al. [2024a], 236 

Cheng et al. [2022], Gentzel et al. [2021]) have also demonstrated the correlation between the agents’ 237 

predictive performance on randomized experimental data and its performance following randomized 238 

deployment. Accordingly, we employ these predictive metrics to approximate randomized post-239 

deployment performance, which can then be integrated into the AI decision via an appropriate utility 240 

function. 241

## 7 Limitation and conclusion 305 

Limitation. First, our theoretical guarantees rely on three assumptions, which may not hold for all 306 

real-world scenes and agents. Second, while our method has been validated across multiple domains, 307 

its generalization in extremely high-dimensional settings (such as LLM) require further investigation. 308 

Third, lower error and tighter bound are required to apply evaluation models into real applications 309 

with small sample. Four, scalability of evaluation models across scenes is another problem in future 310 

study. Five, the post-experiment metric (such as mortality) of randomized deployed mini-agents with 311 

high utility should be collected to increase the value of computational evaluation. 312 

Conclusion. In this work, we propose a novel computational framework for the evaluation of mini-313 

agents that rigorously derives upper bounds on generalized evaluation error and effect error, and 314

results, conducted over 12 diverse scenes—including individual medicine, scientific simulation, social 316 

experiments, business activity, and quantum trade—demonstrate significant error reductions (ranging 317 

from 24.1% to 99.0%) and substantial acceleration (up to 10 7) compared to traditional evaluation 318 

methods. These findings highlight the potential of our technical route to drastically reduce evaluation 319 

costs for rapid model iteration. Future work will focus on more benefit-related metrics, multi-source 320 

inputs, novel neural architectures for evaluation tasks and enhancing the validation of key assumption. 321

## B Investigation of agent evaluation 809 

B.1 Existed agent evaluation works 810 

The theoretical foundations of agent evaluation, particularly with causal guarantees, remain underde-811 

veloped. To date, we are not aware of any systematic theory that rigorously supports causal inference 812 

for agent performance evaluation. While several benchmarking efforts exist, they primarily focus on 813 

performance measurement rather than causal understanding. MLAgentBench (Huang et al. [2024]) 814 

uses 13 tasks (including Canonical Tasks, Classic Kaggle, Kaggle Challenges, Recent Research, and 815 

Code Improvement) to evaluate the performance of large language model. SUPER Benchmark (Bogin 816 

et al. [2024]) is used to evaluate the agents’ ability to reproduce results from research repositories. AI 817 

Clinician (Komorowski et al. [2018]) and AI Hospital (Fan et al. [2025]) use synthetic data to test 818 

the performance of clinical agents. Jia et al. [2024] explores the LLMs’ output decisions’ alignment 819 

with human norms and ethical expectations. However, a common limitation across these works is 820 

the lack of control over unobserved confounders, such as participants’ pre-experiment training or 821 

researchers’ design biases. These hidden common causes may simultaneously influence both the 822 

deployed agent and its measured performance in real-world settings. As a result, these benchmarks 823 

cannot identify the (conditional) causal effect of an agent on real-world outcomes—such as clinical 824 

mortality—limiting their utility for causal evaluation. 825 

B.2 Causal mini agent evaluation and potential outcome 826 

Despite targeting different problem settings, the causal computational evaluation framework and 827 

the potential outcomes framework (Imbens and Rubin [2015]) for agent evaluation share one key 828 

connection and exhibit two fundamental differences. The connection lies in the fact that the uncon-829 

foundedness (ignorability/exchangeability) assumption in potential outcomes can be directly implied 830 

by the IRIS assumption. Furthermore, IRIS can be satisfied in practice by deploying mini agents in a 831 

randomized manner. The first major difference is that the Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption 832 

(no interference and consistency)—a common requirement in potential outcome formulations—is 833 

not necessary under our framework, as long as the IIDE (Independent and Identically Distributed 834 

Evaluations) assumption holds. The second key difference is that the positivity assumption, which 835 

typically requires a non-zero probability of receiving each treatment, is also not required. Never-836 

theless, the causal effect of mini agents on evaluation metrics can still be bounded in the limit, as 837 

formally established in Theorem 4. These distinctions suggest that the proposed framework can be 838 

viewed as a generalized and more flexible extension of the potential outcomes framework, tailored 839 

specifically for the causal evaluation of mini agent behaviors. 840 

B.3 Existed causal inference benchmark and packages 841 

Although a number of benchmark datasets and open-source packages have been developed to address 842 

general causal inference problems—such as CausalML (Zhao and Liu [2023]), EconML (Battocchi 843 

et al. [2019]), DoWhy (Blöbaum et al. [2024], Sharma and Kiciman [2020]), CauseBox (paras2612), 844 

CausalNex (quantumblacklabs), Causal Curve (Kobrosly [2020]), CausalDiscovery (Kalainathan 845 

et al. [2020]), pcalg (Kalisch et al. [2012]), bnlearn (Scutari [2010]), TETRAD (Ramsey et al.), 846 

CausalityBenchmark (Shimoni et al. [2018]), JustCause (inovex), CausalEffect (Tikka and Karvanen 847 

[2017]), Ananke (Lee et al. [2023]), Dagitty (Textor et al. [2011]), YLearn (Bochen Lyu [2022]), 848 

CausalImpact (Google), causal-learn (Zheng et al. [2024]), gCastle (Zhang et al. [2021]), dosearch 849 

(Tikka et al. [2021]), CEE (Li), awesome-causality-identification (YAN [2025]) —these tools are 850 

primarily designed for causal discovery or treatment effect estimation. Despite extensive investigation, 851 

we did not identify any existing algorithms or frameworks that directly address the problem of causal 852 

evaluation of agents, particularly in the context of evaluating agent-driven decision-making processes. 853
