Highlights 

• RISED: a five-dimension pre-deployment evaluation framework for clinical AI 

• Two dimensions fail and one is statistically inconclusive despite AUROC 0.961; re-validated on three real cohorts 

• Bootstrap 95% CIs yield PASS, FAIL, and INCONCLUSIVE verdicts per dimension 

• Reliability is model-dependent; Inclusivity and Sensitivity are data-dependent 

• Numerical readiness gate that licenses entry to silent-trial clinical evaluation 

Abstract 

Aggregate accuracy metrics dominate the evaluation of clinical AI decision-support systems but do not detect deployment-phase failures of input reliability, subgroup equity, threshold sensitivity, or operational feasibility. We propose the RISED Framework : a five-dimension pre-deployment evaluation covering 

Reliability, Inclusivity, Sensitivity, Equity, and Deployability, in which each dimension is operationalized through formal sub-criteria, pre-specified pass/fail thresholds, and bias-corrected accelerated (BCa) bootstrap 95% confidence intervals combined under a Holm-Bonferroni family-wise error correction. A central demonstration is that a classifier satisfying conventional high-discrimination benchmarks can simultaneously fail input-encoding stability and threshold-shift sensitivity checks, while subgroup AUC parity remains statistically inconclusive, pointing to deployment risks that aggregate evaluation alone cannot detect. We validate this differential pass/fail pattern on a synthetic cohort and three publicly available real-world cohorts spanning 35 years of clinical data vintage, from a 1980s cardiology dataset to a 2024 nationally representative health survey; which dimensions fail differs across cohorts, providing preliminary evidence of construct validity. The Equity dimension is reframed as a proxy-dependence diagnostic rather than a stand-alone gate: any need-based fairness verdict computed against a utilization-derived proxy carries a construct-validity problem the framework surfaces explicitly, triggering a procurement requirement for an outcome-independent need measure before the gate is binding. RISED is released as an open-source Python package that supplies the quantitative verdicts existing clinical AI reporting standards require, providing a principled gateway between in-silico model validation and silent-trial clinical evaluation. 

Keywords: clinical AI evaluation; clinical decision support; algorithmic fairness; model reliability; subgroup performance; deployment readiness; pre-deployment testing.

# 1 Introduction 

Artificial intelligence (AI) is increasingly embedded in clinical workflows, from diagnostic decision support and prognostic risk estimation to alert generation and treatment selection. As these systems move from research to deployment, evaluation failures become consequential: a systematic bias or instability at the point of care can directly affect diagnosis, treatment, or workflow (Obermeyer et al., 2019; Finlayson et al., 2021). The expansion of clinical AI has brought parallel evaluation methodology. AI Fairness 360 (Bellamy et al., 2019) and Fairlearn (Bird et al., 2020) package fairness metrics for practitioners; TRIPOD+AI (Collins et al., 2024) provides structured reporting checklists; MI-CLAIM (Norgeot et al., 2020) specifies minimum reporting information for clinical AI; CONSORT-AI (Liu et al., 2020) and SPIRIT-AI (Cruz Rivera et al., 2020) extend trial-reporting standards; and FUTURE-AI (Lekadir et al., 2025) consolidates trustworthy-AI principles. But these resources were built for static model selection or post-hoc reporting, not for determining whether a system is ready to operate reliably and equitably in a live clinical environment (Subbaswamy and Saria, 2021). Deployment introduces conditions that offline benchmarks do not anticipate. Clinically equivalent inputs are often encoded differently across time, clinical site, or electronic health record system, and the encoding shift alone destabilizes predictions (Finlayson et al., 2021). Underrepresented subpopulations receive systematically degraded predictions while aggregate metrics look clean (Obermeyer et al., 2019; Celi et al., 2022). Decision thresholds, routinely retuned in deployment to balance sensitivity and specificity, can substantially change which patients the model flags. Clinicians acting on those flags in real time also need interpretable outputs (Sutton et al., 2020; Rudin, 2019). These failure modes are not hypothetical. Documented examples in the clinical AI literature include systematically degraded subgroup performance under proxy outcomes (Obermeyer et al., 2019), shortcut learning in imaging diagnostics (DeGrave et al., 2021), and encoded bias in clinical natural language processing (Ross et al., 2021). The most cited cautionary deployment case is the Epic Sepsis Model: a proprietary sepsis predictor implemented at hundreds of U.S. hospitals, externally validated by Wong et al. (2021) on 38,455 hospitalizations, was found to have hospitalization-level AUROC of only 0.63, miss 67% of sepsis cases, and generate alerts for 18% of all hospitalizations, producing both poor discrimination and substantial alert fatigue despite passing internal benchmarks (Habib et al., 2021). Epic substantially revised the model in 2022–2023 in response to those critiques; we cite the 2021 incident not as the current state of that product but as the canonical documented case of a deployed clinical AI whose pre-deployment evaluation did not surface its real-world failure modes. As Shah et al. (2019) argue, accuracy metrics that gauge how closely model output matches known labels do not measure whether deployment will improve care; pre-deployment evaluation must therefore look beyond aggregate discrimination. Evaluation frameworks that cannot detect these failure modes before deployment cannot prevent them. We propose the RISED Framework , a five-dimension pre-deployment evaluation approach for clinical AI decision-support systems: 

• Reliability : output stability under semantically equivalent but differently encoded inputs; 

• Inclusivity : performance consistency across demographic subpopulations, via subgroup AUC parity and calibration; 

• Sensitivity : behavioral stability under decision-threshold shifts, measured via decision flip rates; 2• Equity : alignment of model predictions with an independent measure of clinical need, beyond demo-graphic parity; and 

• Deployability : operational feasibility, covering explanation faithfulness and end-to-end latency. Each dimension is operationalized through measurable sub-criteria with explicit mathematical definitions, grounded in the published evaluation and fairness literature. The Reliability dimension’s Judge Sensitivity Score (JSS) is introduced here as a clinical-AI extension of the prompt-sensitivity concept explored for LLM-as-a-judge systems in JudgeSense (Bellibatlu, 2026a). The framework also connects to the broader paradigm of deployment-oriented AI evaluation surveyed in a prior preprint on prompt-engineering evaluation frameworks (Bellibatlu, 2026b). The paper makes four contributions. First, we organize the existing evaluation literature into a five-dimension pre-deployment framework specific to clinical decision-support contexts. Second, each dimension gets a formal sub-criterion, a bootstrap 95% CI, and a PASS / FAIL / INCONCLUSIVE decision rule, replacing qualitative checklists with a quantitative gate. Third, we apply the framework to four cohorts: a 10,000-patient synthetic cohort built on a Synthea-inspired generator (Walonoski et al., 2018); the UCI Heart Disease cohort (Detrano et al., 1989) ( n = 303 , real, 1989); the UCI Diabetes 130-US Hospitals cohort (Strack et al., 2014) ( n = 99 ,492 EHR encounters, real, 1999–2008); and the National Center for Health Statistics National Health Interview Survey 2024 Sample Adult file ( n = 9 ,747 post-cleaning, real, collected calendar year 2024 and released by NCHS in mid-2025; National Center for Health Statistics, 2025). The synthetic cohort isolates the self-derived-outcome circularity question; the two historical UCI cohorts test whether the framework’s verdicts persist outside the synthetic setting; and NHIS 2024 places the verdicts on contemporary, post-pandemic survey data. Fourth, we run Fairlearn (Bird et al., 2023) on the same model and cohort to locate where RISED overlaps with existing fairness toolkits and where it adds something new. Section 2 reviews related evaluation work and identifies the gap RISED addresses. Section 3 formally specifies all five dimensions. Section 4 presents results on a synthetic cohort and a real-data cross-check. Section 5 discusses implications for developers, deployers, and regulators. Section 6 concludes. 

# 2 Background and Related Work 

# 2.1 AI Evaluation in Healthcare 

Clinical machine learning has matured from isolated proof-of-concept studies into routine use across diagnosis, prognosis, and decision-support applications in many specialties (Rajpurkar et al., 2022; Topol, 2019; Liu et al., 2019). Two complementary lines of community infrastructure have followed. On the toolkit side, AI Fairness 360 (Bellamy et al., 2019) and Fairlearn (Bird et al., 2020, 2023) package fairness diagnostics and mitigation algorithms behind a Python API, lowering the threshold for practitioners to compute group-level metrics. On the reporting side, TRIPOD (Collins et al., 2015) and its AI-aware update TRIPOD+AI (Collins et al., 2024) require authors to disclose the ingredients of a prediction-model study (model class, training data, calibration, validation strategy) in a structured form that makes cross-study comparison feasible. A second, more recent strand of work targets the deployment-readiness question more directly. APPRAISE-AI (Kwong et al., 2023) is a quantitative scoring tool that grades AI studies for clinical decision support across six methodological domains; FUTURE-AI (Lekadir et al., 2025) is an international consensus guideline organized around six principles (fairness, universality, traceability, usability, robustness, explainability); and DECIDE-AI (Vasey et al., 2022) is the canonical reporting checklist for the early-stage live clinical 3evaluation of AI-based decision support, sitting between in-silico validation and large-scale trials. RISED is complementary to all three: it adopts the quantitative-gate spirit of APPRAISE-AI but commits to specific metrics, thresholds, and CIs; it shares principles with FUTURE-AI but operationalizes them; and where DECIDE-AI structures the live-evaluation report, RISED specifies the numerical pre-deployment evidence on which that evaluation can build. These tools share a common limitation, though: the dominant evaluation paradigm was built for static model selection, not operational deployment. Evaluation happens once, at development time, on a held-out test set from the same data-generating process as training. The resulting metrics (AUROC, Brier score, calibration plots) answer whether the model ranks patients well within the development sample . They say nothing about whether the model behaves consistently when deployed to a different clinical site, a different EHR version, or a population that has shifted in ways the training data did not capture (Subbaswamy and Saria, 2021; Kelly et al., 2019; Ghassemi et al., 2020). The result, as Kelly et al. (Kelly et al., 2019) observe, is a systematic gap between development-phase accuracy and deployment-phase reliability that existing evaluation practice is not set up to detect. 

# 2.2 Fairness, Equity, and Bias in Clinical AI 

Health disparities propagate through clinical AI systems and can be amplified by them. The canonical demonstration is Obermeyer and colleagues’ analysis (Obermeyer et al., 2019) of a widely deployed risk score that performed well on the loss function it was trained for, yet systematically routed Black patients away from care-management programs because its target variable (annual healthcare spending) was depressed for groups whose access was already constrained. Similar patterns (training-objective accuracy hiding subgroup harm) recur in radiological deep learning (DeGrave et al., 2021), clinical NLP (Ross et al., 2021), and sex/gender-stratified prediction (Cirillo et al., 2020), with systematic reviews (Nazer et al., 2023; Celi et al., 2022) confirming that the issue is structural rather than idiosyncratic to any one application. Population-level outcome heterogeneity across age, sex, and racial/ethnic subgroups (Osibogun, 2024) reinforces the same structural argument from a public-health direction. Machine-learning fairness research has produced a menu of formal criteria that try to capture different moral intuitions about what equality of prediction means: error-rate parity across groups (equalized odds, equal opportunity) (Hardt et al., 2016), group-conditional probability calibration (Pleiss et al., 2017), and similar-treatment-of-similar-individuals (individual fairness) (Dwork et al., 2012). A well-known impossibility result (Chouldechova, 2017) shows that when group base rates differ, these criteria are mutually inconsistent, so any toolkit that operationalizes fairness must pick sides among them. Paulus and Kent (Paulus and Kent, 2020) draw a further distinction that is specifically clinical: statistical demographic parity (equal predicted-positive rates across groups) is a different property from need-based fairness (predictions that track actual disease burden rather than utilization proxies that access barriers distort). This second notion, alignment with clinical need rather than with the historical utilization that already encodes disparity, is what the Equity dimension of RISED operationalizes. Liu and colleagues (Liu et al., 2023) extend this argument: in the clinical setting, equity , not statistical equality , is the appropriate fairness target, and the choice of sensitive variables and fairness metrics must be motivated clinically rather than imported from generic ML-fairness practice. A complementary cautionary finding from medical imaging (Yang et al., 2024) shows that fairness fixes that work in-distribution often fail to generalize: models with the smallest in-sample subgroup gaps were not the most equitable in new test environments. Pfohl et al. (2021) make a related empirical point for clinical risk prediction specifically: penalizing group-fairness violations during training 4tends to degrade within-group performance on multiple metrics, so a fairness verdict cannot be read off a single statistic without examining the trade-off it implies. Both findings argue for evaluating fairness across distributions and across metrics rather than only within a single in-sample summary, which is one of the motivations for running RISED’s Inclusivity dimension on multiple cohorts and reporting the underlying 

∆AUC and ECE components alongside the verdict. 

# 2.3 Reporting Standards and Regulatory Context 

Three jurisdictions have, in different vocabularies, converged on the same broad expectation: clinical AI should not enter routine use without pre-deployment evidence that it is reliable and equitable. The U.S. FDA’s AI/ML-Based Software as a Medical Device Action Plan (U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2021) introduces the concept of a predetermined change-control plan, allowing adaptive models to update post-clearance only along pre-specified trajectories. The Office of the National Coordinator’s HTI-1 rule (Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology, 2024), on the certification side, mandates that algorithmic decision-support tools shipped with certified EHR products expose their inputs, logic, and demographic-subgroup performance to the clinicians using them. On the European side, the AI Act (European Parliament and Council of the European Union, 2024) takes a risk-tiered approach in which AI used to inform clinical decisions is in the high-risk category, triggering conformity-assessment, transparency, and human-oversight obligations as a market-entry condition. Trial-stage reporting standards address the same disclosure problem from the prospective-evaluation side: CONSORT-AI (Liu et al., 2020) and SPIRIT-AI (Cruz Rivera et al., 2020) extend the CONSORT and SPIRIT guidelines to clinical trials of AI interventions, requiring authors to disclose the input handling, performance error analysis, and human-AI interaction protocol of any trialed system. DECIDE-AI (Vasey et al., 2022) sits one stage upstream, structuring the report of early-stage live clinical evaluation between in-silico validation and the larger trials CONSORT-AI / SPIRIT-AI govern. MINIMAR (Hernandez-Boussard et al., 2020) defines the minimum information that must accompany any reported clinical AI model (training population, target population, model architecture, and validation procedure) and is conceptually closest to the disclosure scope that RISED’s per-dimension report fills with numerical content. These reporting frameworks all specify what must appear in a study report; none of them specifies what numerical bar a candidate system must clear before such a study is warranted. RISED targets that latter step. Governance scholarship (Reddy et al., 2020) and the AMIA consensus statement on AI-enabled clinical decision support (Labkoff et al., 2024) both translate these requirements into operational terms. The AMIA statement names the central gap: regulations and reporting standards explain what must be disclosed, but they do not enumerate the specific tests, thresholds, or responsible parties that constitute pre-deployment due diligence. RISED fills that gap. 

# 2.4 Gaps in Existing Frameworks 

Four gaps in current evaluation practice motivated the RISED Framework. Each maps to a corresponding dimension in Section 3. 

First , the standard one-shot held-out evaluation says nothing about how a model behaves when the same clinical reality is encoded differently downstream. A diagnosis coded at a coarser ICD granularity, a lab result reported in different units after a site upgrade, or a comorbidity flag inferred from a slightly different SQL query can all change the input vector for a patient whose clinical state is unchanged (Finlayson et al., 52021; Subbaswamy and Saria, 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Wong et al., 2021). Translational frameworks like TEHAI (Reddy, 2021) have argued that deployment readiness deserves its own evaluation phase, but no existing toolkit, to our knowledge, operationalizes encoding-stability testing as a concrete pre-deployment step. 

Second , most fairness audits stop at aggregate demographic parity without asking whether predictions track actual clinical need (Obermeyer et al., 2019; Paulus and Kent, 2020). The Obermeyer case is precisely the failure mode this leaves uncaught: standard parity checks could not have flagged a model that satisfied calibration within group while still under-scoring the patients with the most unmet need. 

Third , threshold tuning is a routine clinical-deployment step; operators move the cutoff to trade sensitivity for specificity according to local cost structure. But no widely-used evaluation reports how much that tuning would change the patient flag set. The relationship between threshold and reclassification volume has not been systematically benchmarked (Wynants et al., 2020). 

Fourth , operational feasibility properties such as explanation interpretability, explanation consistency across patients, and inference speed at the bedside are typically siloed in the engineering team, while statistical performance is reported by the data science team (Antoniadi et al., 2021; Sutton et al., 2020). For users who have to act on model outputs in real time the separation is unhelpful and, in some deployment patterns, actively unsafe (Rudin, 2019; Sendak et al., 2020). The RISED Framework addresses each of these gaps through a corresponding evaluation dimension. 

# 3 The RISED Framework 

# 3.1 Dimension 1: Reliability 

In practice, a model encounters inputs that are semantically equivalent to training examples yet encoded differently: the same diagnosis at a different ICD granularity across EHR versions, the same lab value in different units across sites, or the same comorbidity captured at a slightly different date. When such encodings produce different model outputs, patients with identical clinical states may be prioritized or deprioritized based on administrative artifacts rather than medical need. A recent scoping review catalogued eight robustness notions in healthcare ML, with input-perturbation stability among the least systematically tested (Balendran et al., 2025). Threat model and scope of the JSS metric. The Reliability dimension is not a certified-robustness or worst-case adversarial analysis in the sense of Madry et al. The package documentation specifies how to construct batteries that approximate site-to-site coding transitions (ICD-9 →ICD-10, LOINC harmonization, unit changes mg/dL ↔mmol/L), which we expect to dominate real deployment-time variation; isotropic Gaussian noise is reported in this paper as a baseline, not as a final clinical battery. 

# 3.2 Dimension 2: Inclusivity 

A model may achieve strong aggregate discrimination while performing markedly worse for specific patient subpopulations. Race, sex, and age are the most consistently documented axes of clinical outcome dispar-ity (Obermeyer et al., 2019; Celi et al., 2022; Osibogun, 2024) and are also those the FDA AI/ML Action Plan (U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2021) and ONC HTI-1 rule (Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology, 2024) require reporting on. We note that race in clinical algorithms is a fraught construct: Vyas et al. (2020) document at least thirteen widely deployed clinical algorithms (eGFR, vaginal-birth-after-cesarean, STONE) whose race-correction terms produced demonstrable patient harm. RISED’s Inclusivity dimension surfaces subgroup-AUC and subgroup-calibration gaps; whether such a gap reflects a genuine biological signal or a structural-inequity artifact remains a domain-expert judgment that the framework cannot make unilaterally. Insurance type is added because access-coverage status encodes utilization-based disparity (Paulus and Kent, 2020); other partitions can be supplied via the package’s user-defined subgroup keys. Beyond discrimination, calibration must hold within subgroups: systematic probability over- or underestimation for a particular group causes incorrect decisions even when aggregate calibration looks clean (Pleiss et al., 2017; Van Calster et al., 2019). The Inclusivity dimension jointly evaluates subgroup discrimination and calibration. # 3.4 Dimension 4: Equity 

Existing fairness criteria (equalized odds, demographic parity, calibration within groups) evaluate the relationship between model predictions and observed outcomes, typically healthcare utilization or cost. As Paulus and Kent (Paulus and Kent, 2020) demonstrate, this approach breaks down when utilization is itself distorted by access barriers: a model can satisfy calibration within race groups while still systematically under-scoring the groups with the greatest unmet clinical need, because their historical cost records understate their true burden of illness. The Equity dimension operationalizes need-based fairness : alignment of model predictions with independent measures of clinical need, rather than with proxies that may encode structural inequity. Let si = f (xi) denote the predicted score and ci denote a clinical need measure (such as a normalized comorbidity count, or the binary label yi as a proxy). Sub-criteria. E1 ρneed ≥ 0.70 : the model’s scores rank patients by clinical need with at least moderate Spearman rank correlation. 

E2 |∆g | ≤ 0.10 for all subgroups g: no subgroup is systematically under- or over-scored by more than 10 percentage points relative to its mean clinical need. 

Diagnostic framing of the Equity verdict. We caution that ρneed is informative only to the degree the chosen need proxy is informationally outside the model’s training feature set . As we show empirically across all four cohorts (§4.4), the verdict under E1 changes when the proxy changes; in the synthetic cohort, for instance, E1 PASSes under the outcome-label proxy but FAILs under the CCI proxy. We therefore 9recommend treating the Equity dimension as a diagnostic of proxy-dependence rather than as a stand-alone gate: an Equity FAIL or proxy-disagreement at the default thresholds should trigger procurement of an outcome-independent need measure (e.g., prospectively recorded nurse acuity, downstream hospitalization within a fixed window after scoring) before deployment, not an automatic stop. The rised package emits a

# 3.6 Default Thresholds and Decision Rule 

Each RISED sub-criterion requires an explicit pass/fail threshold. We provide default thresholds (Table 1) intended as starting points, set a priori using published clinical conventions and author judgment; no data snooping was performed. We do not claim these defaults are universally derived constants. To be candid: the JSS < 0.05 default has no published empirical basis specific to input-perturbation flip rates; we adopt the 5% decision-error tolerance common in clinical prediction model validation (Steyerberg et al., 2010) as a defensible analogue, but the pre-deployment evaluation literature does not yet contain a rigorous empirical 10 study tying a particular flip-rate threshold to clinical harm. This is a gap the framework is intended to provoke empirical work on. Implementations should recalibrate thresholds empirically against deployment outcomes for the target use case; the rised package exposes all thresholds as user parameters. 

Multiple-comparisons control (Holm-Bonferroni). With five dimensions, each contributing one or more sub-criterion tests plus the per-subgroup inclusivity tests, the family of hypotheses tested in a typical RISED run is large; without correction the family-wise false-FAIL rate under the global null exceeds the nominal 5%. We control this by applying the Holm-Bonferroni step-down procedure across the full test family. Tests are ordered by ascending p-value (where the p-value is computed from the BCa bootstrap distribution as the proportion of replicates more extreme than the threshold), and the k-th-smallest p-value is compared against α/ (m − k + 1) , where m is the family size. The headline per-dimension verdicts in Tables 3–6 use both the per-test 95% BCa CI and the family-corrected Holm threshold; a verdict is reported as FAIL only if both rules agree. The package’s holm_bonferroni() helper exposes the correction explicitly and reports per-test adjusted alphas alongside the headline CIs. 

BCa over percentile bootstrap. We use the bias-corrected accelerated (BCa) bootstrap of Efron (1987) rather than the percentile bootstrap. Percentile intervals are known to undercover for metrics bounded near 0 or 1 (JSS, max TFR, parity gaps), and B = 1 ,000 percentile-method bounds have non-trivial Monte Carlo error at the boundary. BCa adjusts the CI endpoints using a bias-correction z0 from the bootstrap distribution and an acceleration a from the leave-one-out jackknife. The full implementation, including the n

jackknife re-evaluations per metric, is in rised/bootstrap_ci.py .

Power and minimum test-set size. Because the per-dimension verdict depends on whether a bootstrap CI brackets a fixed threshold, the test-set size required to deliver an informative (non-INCONCLUSIVE) verdict scales with the magnitude of the true effect. A back-of-the-envelope calculation for the JSS metric, treating each patient’s flip event as Bernoulli with rate p and using the normal approximation to the bootstrap CI, gives a half-CI width of approximately 1.96 √p(1 − p)/n . For a true JSS of 0.05 (right at the threshold), detecting a 0.01 deviation with 80% power requires roughly n ≈ 1,500 patients; for a true JSS of 0.10, the same detection power is achieved at n ≈ 700 . The 2,000-patient test sets used in this paper are therefore adequate to resolve mid-range effects but are at the edge of resolution for borderline cases (the borderline Inclusivity finding on the synthetic cohort, with its [0.042, 0.066] CI, is exactly such a case). The same back-of-envelope applies, with metric-specific variance: for ∆AUC between two equal-size subgroups the half-CI width is approximately 1.96 √2 · Var(AUC) , and using Var (AUC ) ≈ 0.005 for a moderately accurate classifier on ng ≈ 400 subgroups, detecting a 0.01 deviation above the 0.05 cutoff requires n ≳ 3,000 per subgroup; for max TFR the threshold-flip event is again Bernoulli with Var ( TFR ) = p(1 − p)/n , and detecting a 0.01 deviation above the 0.10 threshold requires n ≈ 3,500 . Both metrics are therefore harder to resolve at the threshold than JSS, and INCONCLUSIVE verdicts on small cohorts are expected. Studies aiming for clean PASS/FAIL (rather than INCONCLUSIVE) on small effect sizes should size the test set per metric, not just per dimension. 

Hypothesis framing. Each dimension can be read as a one-sided hypothesis test. For Reliability, 

H0 : JSS ≥ 0.05 (the model is unacceptably input-sensitive) and H1 : JSS < 0.05 . The CI rule we use ( PASS 

if 95% two-sided CI upper bound < threshold) is equivalent to a one-sided test at α = 0 .025 (the upper tail of a two-sided 95% CI corresponds to a 97.5% one-sided UCB). We use this 0.025 level rather than 0.05 

11 deliberately: a deployment-readiness gate should err on the side of declaring INCONCLUSIVE rather than PASS on borderline evidence. The same template applies to Inclusivity ( H0 : ∆ AUC > 0.05 ), Sensitivity (H0 : max TFR > 0.10 ), and (when used as a diagnostic) Equity ( H0 : ρneed < 0.70 ); Deployability latency (H0 : Λ > 500 ms ) is reported without a bootstrap CI because per-call latency is hardware-bounded rather than sample-bounded. INCONCLUSIVE corresponds to the CI bracketing the threshold so that neither one-sided test rejects at α = 0 .025 .

Bootstrap method. All headline CIs use the bias-corrected accelerated (BCa) bootstrap of Efron (1987) with B = 1 ,000 iterations (random state 42), resampling patients with replacement at each iteration to preserve the patient as the unit of statistical independence. Within each bootstrap sample we recompute the full metric (JSS, ∆AUC , max TFR, ρneed ). The BCa endpoints adjust the percentile bounds using a bias-correction z0 (the standard-normal quantile of the fraction of bootstrap replicates below the point estimate) and an acceleration a computed from the leave-one-out jackknife on the same metric. BCa-corrected endpoints have correct coverage in finite samples for metrics bounded near 0 or 1 (JSS, max TFR, parity gap), where the plain percentile bootstrap is known to undercover. The implementation, including the n

jackknife re-evaluations per metric, is in rised/bootstrap_ci.py and is the default for all results reported in this paper. 

CI-based decision rule. For dimensions where a bootstrap 95% confidence interval is computed (Reliability JSS, Inclusivity ∆AUC , Sensitivity max TFR), we apply the following CI-aware decision rule rather than relying on the point estimate alone: 

• PASS if the 95% BCa CI upper bound is below the threshold; 

• FAIL if the 95% BCa CI lower bound is above the threshold; 

• INCONCLUSIVE otherwise (the CI brackets the threshold); 

• DIAGNOSTIC (Equity only) when the dimension is configured to report a proxy-dependence diagnostic rather than a stand-alone gate. A DIAGNOSTIC verdict is never combined into the all-pass gating conjunction (§3.4) regardless of whether the underlying ρneed would individually PASS, FAIL, or be INCONCLUSIVE under any chosen proxy. The rationale is construct-validity: across all four cohorts in this paper, the ρneed verdict changes when the proxy changes, so we report both proxy-specific point estimates and CIs but treat the dimension as informative-only until an outcome-independent need measure is available. Operational consequence: a DIAGNOSTIC verdict triggers a procurement requirement, not a deployment block. When Equity is DIAGNOSTIC under the default proxies, the deployer must obtain an outcome-independent need measure (e.g., prospectively recorded nurse acuity scores, downstream hospitalization within a fixed window after scoring) and re-run E1/E2 against it; only then is the binding gate complete. Without that measure, downstream deployment evidence remains incomplete by construction. The INCONCLUSIVE category is methodologically more honest than treating a borderline point estimate as a definitive verdict and signals that a larger test set or additional data is needed to resolve the uncertainty. 

Threshold sensitivity and metric monotonicity. We check verdict robustness in two ways. (a) Threshold sweep: the Reliability verdict is FAIL at thresholds 0.025–0.050, INCONCLUSIVE at 0.075, and PASS only at 0.10. Sensitivity is FAIL across the full 5%–15% band. (b) Monotonicity: under Gaussian noise 12 σ ∈ { 0, 0.025 , 0.05 , 0.10 }, JSS increases near-monotonically from 0% to ∼10% , confirming the metric captures input sensitivity in the expected direction. # 4.1 Data: Synthetic Clinical Cohort 

We generated a synthetic cohort of 10,000 patients using a Synthea-inspired generative model (Walonoski et al., 2018) implemented in the released rised.datasets module. No real patient records were used at any stage. The cohort spans a demographically and clinically heterogeneous adult population and is released openly to support reproducibility (Section ?? ). 

Demographic composition. Age distribution was weighted toward adults: 18.4% aged 18–44, 25.0% aged 45–64, 28.2% aged 65–74, and 28.4% aged 75 and older. Sex distribution was 55.5% female and 44.5% male. Race/ethnicity composition was 63.8% White, 13.4% Black, 13.0% Hispanic, 5.7% Asian, and 4.1% Other. An insurance-type variable (47.4% Medicare, 29.8% Private, 14.4% Medicaid, 8.4% Uninsured) was included as a demographic axis for subgroup-performance evaluation. 13 Clinical features. # 4.3 RISED Evaluation Results 

We applied evaluate_all() to the 2,000-patient held-out test set using four perturbation specifications, chosen to mirror clinically realistic encoding-shift mechanisms. Additive Gaussian feature noise at scales 

σ = 0 .05 (5%) and σ = 0 .10 (10%) approximates measurement-noise differences between sites and assay platforms (e.g., variation in lab measurement precision, differing devices for vital signs). Uniform age-feature rescalings at factors 1.05 (+5% ) and 1.06 (+6% ) approximate unit-change and granularity-change effects on numeric features (e.g., year of birth versus age in years, or age-at-encounter versus age-at-enrollment). The specific perturbation set is configurable via evaluate_all() ; practitioners are expected to choose perturbations that reflect the encoding variability of their target deployment environment (ICD-9 vs. ICD-10, mg/dL vs. mmol/L, etc.). A 17-point threshold sweep from τ = 0 .10 to τ = 0 .90 was used for the Sensitivity dimension ( τ0 = 0 .50 ). Under BCa-CIs and the CI-based decision rule, two RISED dimensions fail (Reliability, Sen-sitivity) and one is statistically inconclusive (Inclusivity) on a model with AUROC 0.961, numbers that 17 would conventionally signal a deployment-ready system (Table 3, Figure 6). # 4.4 External Validation on Three Real-Data Cohorts 

To address the construct-validity concern that the synthetic cohort’s findings may be artifacts of a self-derived outcome label, we re-ran the full RISED Framework on three publicly available real-data cohorts spanning very different vintage, scale, and prediction problem: the UCI Heart Disease cohort (1989), the UCI Diabetes 130-US Hospitals cohort (1999–2008), and the contemporary NCHS NHIS 2024 Sample Adult public-use file (collected 2024, released by NCHS in mid-2025). The 35-year vintage span is deliberate: if the framework’s verdicts are artifacts of a particular coding era, they should fail to replicate across this span. 

Cohort A: UCI Heart Disease (Cleveland), n = 303 . A real (non-synthetic) clinical dataset (Detrano et al., 1989) accessed via OpenML, with 13 features (demographics, vital signs, ECG and stress-test results) and a binary outcome (presence of heart disease). XGBoost trained with the same hyperparameters as Section 4.2 on an 80/20 stratified split. The cohort’s small size ( n = 303 ) and the resulting small per-subgroup samples in the test split ( n = 61 ) mean that subgroup metrics on this cohort are inherently noisy, and the wide CI on the Inclusivity verdict (Table 4, [0.001, 0.120]) reflects this directly. We therefore treat the Cleveland scorecard as a directional sanity check that the framework’s pipeline runs on real data and produces readable verdicts, not as a definitive subgroup audit; the larger Diabetes 130 cohort serves the latter role. 

Cohort B: UCI Diabetes 130-US Hospitals, n = 99 ,492 . A real EHR-derived clinical dataset (Strack et al., 2014) of 130 US hospital encounters between 1999 and 2008, accessed via OpenML. The Equity dimension is reported as a diagnostic: the two recommended proxies disagree dramatically (ρneed = 0 .149 under the outcome-label proxy vs. 0.762 under inpatient-utilization), which is exactly the 19 proxy-underdetermination signal the diagnostic is designed to surface; we therefore do not treat it as a stand-alone gate verdict. This contradicts a hypothetical “RISED-just-fails-everything” criticism: the dimensions are empirically separable, and a model can be highly stable to input encoding and still be unfit on every other axis. 

(ii) The Inclusivity failure is large and clinically meaningful. A 0.26 AUC parity gap across race, gender, and age-decade subgroups means the model that ranks readmission risk well in some groups ranks essentially randomly in others, a deployment hazard that standard aggregate evaluation would mask. 

(iii) The Equity dimension’s proxy dependence is again visible but with an opposite sign relative to the synthetic cohort: here the ytrue -based ρneed is low (0.149), simply because the model’s discriminative power is low (AUROC 0.636), while a prior-utilization proxy ( ninpatient , prior inpatient admissions) yields a high 

ρneed of 0.762 because that proxy is included in the feature set and so is mechanically correlated with the score. The disagreement between proxies is the canonical DIAGNOSTIC signal that triggers the procurement requirement for an outcome-independent need measure (§3.6). 

Cohort C: NCHS NHIS 2024 Sample Adult ( n = 9 ,747 ). A reviewer might object that both UCI cohorts pre-date modern EHR infrastructure by 15–35 years and so cannot speak to contemporary clinical-AI deployment. To address this directly, we re-ran the unchanged framework on the National Health Interview Survey 2024 Sample Adult public-use file (National Center for Health Statistics, 2025), collected by the National Center for Health Statistics during calendar year 2024 and released as a CSV public-use file in mid-2025 (we used the file dated 30 June 2025). NHIS is the principal source of information on the health of the U.S. civilian non-institutionalized population. Vintage spans 35 years (Cleveland 1989 → Diabetes 130 1999–2008 → NHIS 2024), so the framework’s verdicts cannot be attributed to a single coding-era artifact. The Cleveland cohort’s small sample makes it suggestive but not definitive; Diabetes 130, with n ≈ 10 5 and an EHR-derived readmission outcome, brings the analysis closer to realistic clinical-AI deployment; NHIS 2024 adds a contemporary nationally representative national-survey check on the same dimensions.

Under BCa CIs and the CI-based decision rule, two RISED gating dimensions failed (Reliability, Sensitivity) and one was statistically inconclusive (Inclusivity) on a model with AUROC 0.961, numbers that would typically signal a deployment-ready system. The Equity dimension is reported as a proxy-dependence diagnostic, not as a stand-alone gate. The dimension-level findings below discuss what each non-PASS verdict implies operationally. 

Reliability. A JSS of 0.064 means roughly one in fifteen patients would be reclassified by an encoding change that preserves clinical state, a documented mechanism behind cross-site performance drops (Finlayson et al., 2021; Wong et al., 2021). The 0.05 threshold is the 5% decision-error convention from clinical prediction model validation (Steyerberg et al., 2010); it is a starting point, not a derived constant. 

Inclusivity. The AUC parity gap is concentrated in patients aged 75+, the subgroup with the highest comorbidity burden and the dominant age stratum among positive-outcome patients. Subgroup calibration held (ECE ≤ 0.10 for all groups, with Asian-subgroup ECE = 0 .097 approaching the limit), so the deviation reflects ranking degradation rather than probability miscalibration. The point estimate ∆AUC = 0 .0588 

exceeds the threshold, but the BCa 95% CI [0.042, 0.066] brackets the 0.05 cutoff, so under the CI-based rule the verdict is INCONCLUSIVE (not FAIL). A larger test set or a clinically harder outcome would be required to resolve PASS-vs-FAIL. The 0.05 default aligns with FDA AI/ML Action Plan requirements (U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2021) and is operationalized in AIF360 (Bellamy et al., 2019). 

Sensitivity. A max TFR of 19.9% at τ = 0 .10 means a threshold shift to reduce alert burden would reclassify nearly one in five patients. Threshold selection is non-arbitrary (Wynants et al., 2015); RISED operationalizes quantifying that reclassification as a gateable pre-deployment criterion. 

Equity and Deployability. The proxy-dependent Equity verdict ( ρneed = 0 .732 under the outcome label vs. 0.599 under CCI) is a diagnostic signal, not a methodological failure: it tells the deployer that the question of whether the model serves clinical need is currently unanswerable from the available data and that an outcome-independent need measure is required. The package emits a warning when the outcome label is used as the proxy. Deployability passed at ≈1–2 ms batch latency and Fexpl = 0 .86 . The 500 ms threshold is generous; tighter per-patient thresholds (e.g., 1 ms) may be warranted in time-critical contexts, and the package reports both per-cohort and per-patient latency. 

Positioning with existing frameworks. RISED adds four dimensions (Reliability, Sensitivity, need-aligned Equity, Deployability) that AIF360 (Bellamy et al., 2019) and Fairlearn (Bird et al., 2020, 2023) do not measure; its Inclusivity dimension overlaps with them on subgroup AUC. TRIPOD+AI (Collins et al., 2024) regulates how prediction-model studies are reported ; RISED specifies the numerical evidence the report should contain on deployment readiness. FUTURE-AI (Lekadir et al., 2025) shares RISED’s vocabulary across six principles but recommends practices rather than committing to specific metrics and thresholds. APPRAISE-AI (Kwong et al., 2023) grades the study for methodological quality; RISED grades the model on a held-out cohort. The two are complementary: APPRAISE-AI screens whether a study is credible; RISED tests whether the artifact clears specific operational bars. TEHAI (Reddy, 2021) is a qualitative checklist for deployment readiness; RISED supplies the measurable answers with thresholds and CIs. The Model Facts 28 paradigm (Sendak et al., 2020) is a clinician-facing label format; RISED is the test battery whose outputs would populate it. 

Governance and adoption. A test battery is only as useful as the governance structure that operationalizes it. Whether a RISED FAIL verdict blocks deployment depends on whether the deploying organization treats it as binding rather than advisory. The HTI-1 rule (Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology, 2024) and the EU AI Act conformity-assessment mechanism (European Parliament and Council of the European Union, 2024) create plausible attachment points where a RISED scorecard could be required as part of an evidence package, but the framework cannot mandate enforcement. Governance fit is an empirical question for deployment-team studies that should follow this work. 

Where RISED sits in the AI clinical lifecycle. The translational-AI literature distinguishes in-silico validation, silent-trial evaluation (model runs on live data without acting on care), and prospective clinical evaluation under DECIDE-AI (Vasey et al., 2022) and ultimately CONSORT-AI / SPIRIT-AI (Liu et al., 2020; Cruz Rivera et al., 2020). McCradden et al. (2022) formalize this as a three-stage research-ethics pathway; You et al. (2025) propose safety, efficacy, effectiveness, and monitoring phases. RISED targets the boundary between the first and second stages: the numerical readiness check that licenses a model to enter silent-trial evaluation. Implementation experience from Sepsis Watch (Sandhu et al., 2020) illustrates why a RISED PASS is necessary but not sufficient: clinician trust, communication, and workflow integration determined whether a credible model produced clinical benefit. A model that fails RISED should not be deployed; a model that passes still requires the implementation and governance scaffolding that the surrounding literature describes. 

Limitations. Seven limitations bear on how these results should be read. First, the primary demonstration uses a fully synthetic cohort; the three real-data validations span thirty-five years of vintage (Cleveland 1989, Diabetes 130 1999–2008, NHIS 2024) and the contemporary NHIS 2024 release directly addresses the historical-coding concern, but NHIS is survey rather than EHR data, so a true contemporary EHR cohort (e.g., MIMIC-IV with PhysioNet credentialing) remains outstanding before the framework’s thresholds can be considered clinically calibrated against deployed-model behavior. Second, the Equity dimension’s 

ρneed remains confounded when the proxy and outcome share upstream determinants; we have therefore reframed Equity as a proxy-dependence diagnostic rather than a stand-alone gating dimension, but an outcome-independent need measure (post-discharge mortality, nurse-assessed acuity) would still provide cleaner evaluation. Third, default thresholds were set a priori from published conventions, not from empirical calibration against deployment outcomes; all thresholds are user-configurable in the package. Fourth, the robustness check covers tree-based and linear classifiers; neural architectures and head-to-head benchmarking against AI Fairness 360 on contemporary cohorts remain outstanding. Fifth, the framework targets binary classification; multi-class, ordinal, and time-to-event extensions are left to future work. Sixth, this is a single-author methods contribution; clinical face validity and deployment realism should be assessed in collaboration with clinical informatics teams before RISED is treated as a regulatory artifact. Seventh, the JSS bootstrap CI captures patient-resampling variance but not variance with respect to the composition of the perturbation battery Φ; reported JSS values therefore remain conditional on the specific battery (Gaussian noise σ ∈ { 0.05 , 0.10 } and age rescalings {1.05 , 1.06 } for the synthetic cohort). A clinically realistic battery using ICD-9 →ICD-10 transitions, LOINC harmonization, and unit changes (mg/dL ↔mmol/L) would yield 29 different JSS values; we treat these batteries as configurable inputs and recommend deployers report their own batteries alongside the JSS. 

Regulatory alignment and future directions. The FDA AI/ML Action Plan (U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2021) is a programmatic plan rather than a binding regulation; its operational successors are the FDA Predetermined Change Control Plan and the 2024 lifecycle guidance, both of which require manufacturers to specify, but do not themselves prescribe, the tests that establish reliability and equity. The ONC HTI-1 rule (Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology, 2024) requires 

transparency and risk-management disclosure for Predictive DSI rather than numerical pass/fail bars, and the EU AI Act (European Parliament and Council of the European Union, 2024) Articles 9–15 require risk-management documentation rather than a specific test battery. RISED is therefore positioned not as filling a regulatory gap but as producing the kind of structured numerical evidence that can populate FDA PCCP submissions, HTI-1 risk-management disclosures, and EU AI Act technical files; standardisation of such test batteries is the province of bodies such as ISO/IEC 42001 and AAMI CR-34971. Remaining work includes: (a) validation on contemporary EHR cohorts (MIMIC-IV, eICU); (b) empirically deriving threshold defaults from deployment outcomes rather than convention; (c) head-to-head benchmarking against AIF360, TEHAI, and FUTURE-AI on shared real cohorts; (d) extending dimensions to multi-class, ordinal, and time-to-event outputs; and (e) mapping RISED verdicts onto FDA SaMD pre-market submission artifacts. 

# 6 Conclusion 

Aggregate performance metrics miss reliability failures, subgroup disparities, threshold instability, and operational misalignment. This gap is not hypothetical: it has been documented in commercial clinical AI (Obermeyer et al., 2019), imaging AI (DeGrave et al., 2021), and clinical NLP (Ross et al., 2021), yet existing evaluation practice has no structured pre-deployment mechanism for catching these failures. RISED is a five-dimension pre-deployment evaluation framework with formally specified sub-criteria, default pass/fail thresholds, bootstrap 95% confidence intervals, a CI-based decision rule, and an open-source Python implementation. Each dimension (Reliability, Inclusivity, Sensitivity, Equity, Deployability) addresses a class of deployment-phase failures that standard aggregate benchmarking does not capture. On a synthetic cohort where XGBoost achieves AUROC 0.961, two RISED dimensions failed under the BCa-CI rule and one was inconclusive: JSS = 0.064 (95% BCa CI 0.058–0.070, FAIL); max TFR = 19.9% (95% BCa CI 18.3%–21.7%, FAIL); ∆AUC = 0 .059 (95% BCa CI 0.042–0.066, INCONCLUSIVE: the CI spans the threshold). Equity is reported as a proxy-dependence diagnostic rather than a stand-alone gate; both proxies disagreed (outcome-label ρneed = 0 .732 , PASS; CCI-based ρneed = 0 .599 , FAIL), the canonical signal of need-proxy underdetermination. The same framework applied to three real cohorts yielded non-uniform pass/fail patterns: Reliability passed on Diabetes 130 by three orders of magnitude while Inclusivity and Sensitivity failed decisively; on the contemporary NCHS NHIS 2024 cohort ( n = 9 ,747 , collected calendar year 2024 and released by NCHS in mid-2025), Reliability passed (JSS = 0.011) while Inclusivity ( ∆AUC = 0 .328 )and Sensitivity (max TFR 22.5%) failed decisively, mirroring the Diabetes 130 pattern on data collected three decades later. The Equity proxy-dependence signal recurred across cohorts. Together the four scorecards provide preliminary evidence consistent with construct validity. A multi-model robustness check showed Inclusivity and Sensitivity failures are data-driven and recur across classifiers, while Reliability failure is model-dependent. 30 RISED surfaces deployment-relevant failures that aggregate metrics miss, making it a structured comple-ment to existing evaluation standards including TRIPOD+AI (Collins et al., 2024), MI-CLAIM (Norgeot et al., 2020), and FUTURE-AI (Lekadir et al., 2025). The stronger claim, demonstrated clinical impact, requires prospective deployment evidence, which remains the most important next step. The framework is intended to produce structured numerical evidence that can be incorporated into FDA PCCP submissions (U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2021), HTI-1 risk-management disclosures (Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology, 2024), and EU AI Act technical files (European Parliament and Council of the European Union, 2024); it does not by itself satisfy any of those instruments’ requirements. The rised Python package and the synthetic evaluation cohort are openly available at https://github. com/rohithreddybc/rised-healthcare-eval (MIT license) and https://huggingface.co/datasets/Rohithreddybc/ rised-healthcare-eval-dataset (DOI: 10.57967/hf/8734).

# Ethics Statement 

The primary demonstration cohort is entirely synthetic, generated by a computational model inspired by the Synthea open-source health records simulator. The three real-data validation cohorts (UCI Cleveland Heart Disease, UCI Diabetes 130-US Hospitals, and the NCHS NHIS 2024 Sample Adult public-use file) are de-identified public datasets distributed under their respective release terms; no patient records were re-identified, no additional patient data were collected, and no protected health information was accessed at any stage of this research. Accordingly, no new Institutional Review Board (IRB) approval or informed patient consent was required for the analyses presented here. 31 Computational Reproducibility 

All experimental results in this paper are generated by running the released rised package (MIT license; GitHub link in Code Availability). Exact environment used for the reported numbers: Python 3.11.7, scikit-learn 1.8.0, NumPy 1.26.4, pandas 3.0.2, SciPy 1.15.3, XGBoost 3.2.0, SHAP 0.51.0, Fairlearn 0.13.0, on a laptop with a 13th-generation Intel Core i5-13420H CPU and 16 GB RAM running Windows 11 Home. All training and evaluation calls use a fixed random seed ( random_state=42 ); bootstrap CIs use B = 1 ,000 

iterations with the same seed. The synthetic cohort is generated from a deterministic seeded process (also seed 42); reseeding the entire pipeline on the same hardware and software stack reproduces every reported number to within Monte Carlo bootstrap error ( B = 1 ,000 , seed-fixed). Cross-platform reproducibility is not bit-exact because XGBoost histogram parallelism and SHAP TreeExplainer tied-feature break ordering are non-deterministic across CPU microarchitectures; the verdicts (PASS/FAIL/INCONCLUSIVE) are stable in our cross-machine spot checks but headline metrics may differ in the third decimal place. The full evaluation on the synthetic cohort runs in under one minute end-to-end; the multi-model robustness comparison (Section 4.5) and the UCI Diabetes 130-US Hospitals validation (Section 4.4) each take approximately five to ten minutes on the same hardware.
