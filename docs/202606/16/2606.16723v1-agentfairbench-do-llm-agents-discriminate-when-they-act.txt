ABSTRACT Large language model (LLM) agents increasingly take actions (screening applicants, recom-mending credit, triaging patients), yet fairness for LLMs is still measured by grading answers . We introduce AgentFairBench, a cheap, reproducible, multi-domain benchmark for demographic disparity in the actions of LLM agents. Grounded in a companion framework, the Bias Conduction Framework (BCF, restated here), it spans three regulator-anchored domains: hiring, lending, and medical triage. Synthetic, demographic-neutral profiles are evaluated in counterfactual matched sets that vary only a name-coded race ×gender signal (in the Bertrand–Mullainathan tradition), under four agent scaffolds of increasing agency (direct, chain-of-thought, multi-agent deliberation, tool-augmented). A NumPy-only harness computes counterfactual flip rate, mean absolute score difference (MASD), action-rate disparity, and tool-invocation disparity, with bootstrap confidence intervals, paired tests, and false-discovery-rate control, for single-digit dollars per model. A live leaderboard with a held-out private split and a contamination canary admits external models by submission. Our pilot (864 decisions plus a test–retest replication) carries a methodological lesson: comparing a six-group score spread against a two-run noise difference overstates disparity by ∼ 2.4×

through statistic arity alone. Against an arity-matched noise floor and an omnibus group test, claude-haiku-4-5 shows no demographic effect above sampling noise (0 of 120 pairwise and 0 of 9 omnibus contrasts survive correction); a planted-bias test confirms the instrument detects disparity when present. The contribution is a sound, sensitive, adoption-ready instrument, the arity-matched-null methodology, and open artifacts to scale it. Code, data, and harness are released under open licenses, with an anonymized review artifact. 

INDEX TERMS Algorithmic fairness, large language model agents, counterfactual fairness, demographic disparity, agent evaluation, responsible AI. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Large language models have crossed a threshold: they no longer merely answer questions, they act . LLM-driven agents now screen job applicants, recommend credit decisions, and triage clinical cases (settings in which a biased action changes a person’s access to employment, capital, or care) [1]. Yet the dominant framework for measuring fairness in language models still grades answers : it scores the words a model emits in response to a prompt [2]–[4]. There is a structural gap between what we measure and what now matters. This gap has a precise theoretical cause. The Bias Conduc-tion Framework (BCF), a companion theoretical framework restated self-containedly in Section III, shows that token-level parity can coexist with action-level disparity (Proposition P2, 

Masking ): a model that answers demographic probes even-handedly can still take systematically different actions when those answers are operationalized into decisions, tool calls, and multi-step trajectories. The situation compounds. The same machinery that makes an agent more capable (chain-of-thought, multi-agent deliberation, tool use) can amplify rather than wash out an entering disparity (Proposition P3, Super-additivity ). Existing benchmarks see none of this. Answer-level fairness benchmarks do not act, single-domain agent au-dits cover one vertical, and capability-oriented agent bench-marks [5]–[8] are not demographically instrumented. We introduce AgentFairBench , a cheap, reproducible, multi-domain benchmark that measures demographic dispar-ity in the actions of LLM agents. AgentFairBench opera-tionalizes the BCF’s component counterfactual disparity at the action level across three consequential domains: hiring,

> II. RELATED WORK
> A. ANSWER-LEVEL LLM FAIRNESS BENCHMARKS

A substantial body of work evaluates demographic bias by examining what language models say in response to carefully constructed prompts. BBQ [2] probes social biases across nine protected categories using ambiguous question–answer pairs drawn from a templated question-answering format; StereoSet [3] measures stereotypical associations at the word and sentence level via intrasentence and intersentence con-texts; and CrowS-Pairs [4] presents masked-language-model scoring over minimally contrastive stereotype/anti-stereotype sentence pairs. These benchmarks have produced real insight into the distributional biases encoded during pretraining and instruction tuning. The limits of this answer-level paradigm, and the gap between measuring representational bias and measuring downstream allocative harm, are surveyed criti-cally by Blodgett et al. [12], whose call to center the harms systems actually cause motivates our shift from answers to actions; the same limits are catalogued comprehensively in the bias-and-fairness survey of Gallegos et al. [13]. Each benchmark grades answers , though. The unit of mea-surement is a model’s selection among textual options or its assignment of probability mass to a token sequence. None of them activates an action loop. When a model answers ‘‘yes’’ or ‘‘no’’ to a stereotype probe, no credit score changes, no interview invitation is issued, and no triage escalation is triggered. As formalized in the Bias Conduction Framework’s Proposition P2 (Masking), token-level parity can coexist with action-level disparity because the mapping from language to executable decisions is non-linear and context-dependent. AgentFairBench targets precisely this measurement gap: the question is not what a model says about a demographic group, but what it does when acting consequentially on behalf of one. 

> B. DECISION-LEVEL LLM AUDITS

A growing body of work moves from grading answers to probing the decisions LLMs make under demographic pertur-bation, and is the closest neighbor to our hiring and lending domains. Tamkin et al. [14] introduce DiscrimEval, gener-ating 70 high-stakes decision scenarios across society and systematically varying demographic attributes to measure both positive and negative discrimination in a production model; this is the canonical demonstration that demographi-cally perturbed, decision-level evaluation surfaces disparities that answer-grading misses, and it anticipates the explicit-attribute robustness arm we defer to future work. In the hiring vertical specifically, An et al. [15] score roughly 361,000 re-sumes with randomized social identities across several com-mercial LLMs and find systematic, direction-varying dispar-ities (higher scores for female candidates, lower for Black male candidates), while Haim et al. [16] audit name-coded race and gender across 42 prompt templates and find that Black-women-coded names receive the least advantageous advice. AgentFairBench differs from this line on three axes the comparison below makes precise: these studies elicit a single-shot decision, whereas we wrap each decision in an agent loop and stratify by scaffold depth (C0–C4); they cover one setting each, whereas we span three regulator-anchored domains under one harness; and none instruments the tool-invocation channel ( ∆tool ) that only an action loop exposes. DiscrimEval is the most direct decision-level comparator; it is single-shot and not scaffold-stratified, which is exactly the

> C. SINGLE-DOMAIN AND CLINICAL AGENT FAIRNESS

A newer wave of work moves closer to agents but remains domain-specific. MedAgentBench [17] instruments clinical reasoning tasks for LLM agents, though its primary focus is task capability rather than demographic disparity in out-comes. FairMedQA [18] examines demographic fairness in medical question answering using a counterfactual perturba-tion design similar in spirit to ours, applied to QA accuracy 

(answer-level) within a single clinical vertical and without an agentic action loop. The measurement level (executable actions), not the counterfactual methodology, is our key dif-ferentiator. MFARM [19] evaluates multi-faceted clinical-fairness harms on MIMIC-IV but is restricted to a single domain and a narrow metric set. Most directly, EQUITRIAGE [20] audits gender bias in LLM-based emergency-department triage using counterfactual gender-swaps and flip-rate metrics over MIMIC-IV-ED vignettes. In a dedicated single-domain study it finds measurable action-level disparity in LLM triage, which is concurrent and independent evidence that the triage setting we include is a live one. It remains single-domain (triage) and single-attribute (gender); AgentFairBench adds synthetic, PHI-free reproducible profiles, a race-by-gender rather than gender-only signal, and the cross-domain and scaffold axes. The most closely related prior work is that of Mayil-vaghanan et al. [21], which introduced counterfactual flip rate (CFR) and mean absolute score difference (MASD) as complementary metrics for measuring demographic dis-parity in contact-center quality-assurance agents. That work demonstrated that binary decision disparity (CFR) and graded score disparity (MASD) are not redundant, which is a direct empirical instantiation of the P2 Masking proposition. We adopt CFR and MASD directly from this work, together with the Bertrand–Mullainathan name-substitution paradigm it employs; we claim novelty in neither, and our contribution is their generalization beyond a single contact-center quality-assurance setting. AgentFairBench extends this foundation in three directions: (i) it generalizes across three consequentially distinct domains (hiring, lending, and medical triage) each anchored to a real regulatory standard, rather than a single vertical; (ii) it introduces a scaffold axis (direct, chain-of-thought, multi-agent deliberation, and tool-augmented) that operationalizes the BCF conduction operators, tests the P3 Super-additivity proposition, and yields a tool-invocation dis-parity metric ( ∆tool ) absent from prior work; and (iii) it adds an anti-gaming evaluation protocol (held-out private split, contamination canary, content hashes) delivered through a live leaderboard. 

> D. AGENT CAPABILITY BENCHMARKS

Parallel to fairness-oriented work, the agent community has produced a rich set of capability benchmarks. AgentBench [5] evaluates LLM agents across a diverse suite of interactive environments including web browsing, database manipula-tion, and lateral-thinking games. WebArena [6] provides a realistic, self-hosted web environment in which agents com-plete multi-step tasks on live-feeling websites. SWE-bench [7] measures an agent’s ability to resolve real GitHub is-sues from open-source repositories. Mind2Web [8] focuses on generalist web agents navigating diverse websites from human-annotated demonstrations. These benchmarks are methodologically rigorous and eco-logically valid, requiring genuine multi-step reasoning and tool use. None of them is demographically instrumented. Task profiles are not systematically varied across protected-attribute signals, no counterfactual matched sets are con-structed, and no fairness metrics are computed. A model that achieves state-of-the-art task-completion rates on SWE-bench or WebArena may nonetheless exhibit substantial de-mographic disparity when its actions impinge on hiring, credit, or healthcare decisions. Capability and fairness are orthogonal axes, and existing capability benchmarks measure only one of them. 

> E. POSITIONING AGENTFAIRBENCH

AgentFairBench shares two ingredients with Mayilvaghanan et al. [21]: the CFR/MASD metric pair and the Bertrand– Mullainathan counterfactual design [1]. We claim novelty in neither. Its contribution is a combination that, to our knowl-edge, no prior fairness benchmark provides together. The headline novelty is a scaffold-amplification axis that opera-tionalizes BCF super-additivity and yields the tool-invocation disparity metric ∆tool ; neither the scaffold axis nor ∆tool has any precedent in [21] or the other cited benchmarks. On that we add action-level measurement across multiple con-sequential domains with heterogeneous regulatory anchors, and an anti-gaming evaluation protocol (held-out private split, contamination canary, content hashes) delivered through a live leaderboard. Multi-domain coverage on its own is closer to engineering than to a conceptual advance, which is why we lead with the scaffold axis and ∆tool rather than with breadth. The comparison below situates AgentFairBench against rep-resentative prior benchmarks along these axes. 

> III. BACKGROUND: THE BIAS CONDUCTION FRAMEWORK

Measuring demographic disparity in LLM-agent behavior re-quires a formalism that goes beyond static classifier fairness. The Bias Conduction Framework (BCF) addresses this by modeling an agent as a four-tuple ⟨π, T , M, R⟩ , where π

is the policy (the underlying language model), T is the task specification, M is the working memory (context window, tool outputs, scratchpad), and R is the repertoire of available actions and tools (D1). This formulation treats the agent loop as an object of fairness analysis in its own right, distinct from the model weights alone. D2: Counterfactual Component Disparity. For any com-ponent c ∈ { policy, memory, tools, scaffolding }, the BCF defines counterfactual disparity ∆c via trace-replay: hold all inputs constant except the demographic signal embedded in component c, re-execute the agent trace, and measure the resulting divergence in output distributions. Formally, 

∆c = E[δ(ag, ag0 )] , where ag is the action taken under demographic group g, ag0 is the action under reference group 

g0 (here, white male), and δ is a task-appropriate discrepancy metric (flip rate or score difference). This operationalization draws on the individual-fairness [22], group-fairness [23], and counterfactual-fairness [24] traditions; counterfactual fairness in particular requires that individuals differing only in protected attributes receive equivalent treatment. Agent-FairBench is the executable instantiation of D2 at the action level: counterfactual matched sets vary only a name-coded race-by-gender signal, so any observed ∆c is attributable to the demographic perturbation rather than confounding pro-file content. We note that our matched sets implement a 

correspondence-audit counterfactual (a ceteris-paribus name substitution in the Bertrand–Mullainathan sense), which is related to but not identical with the structural-causal-model counterfactual of [24]: we intervene on a surface proxy for perceived demographics, not on a protected-attribute node in a causal graph, and we therefore do not claim the stronger SCM-counterfactual guarantee. 

D3: Conduction Operators. The BCF characterizes how disparity propagates through the agent pipeline via four operators applied at each component boundary: ATTENU-ATE (downstream processing reduces disparity), PRESERVE (disparity passes through unchanged), AMPLIFY (disparity grows), and GENERATE (disparity is introduced de novo by a component that was itself neutral). These operators supply a mechanistic vocabulary for diagnosis. A scaffold that adds deliberation steps may AMPLIFY disparity already present in the policy, or may GENERATE new disparity through asymmetric tool invocation patterns. 

P2: Masking. Token-level output parity (e.g., equivalent next-token distributions across demographic conditions) does not imply action-level parity. A model may assign near-identical log-probabilities to ‘‘yes’’ and ‘‘no’’ continuations across demographic groups while still producing systemati-cally different discrete decisions after sampling and parsing. This measurement gap, which existing answer-level bench-marks [2]–[4] are structurally unable to detect, motivates grounding evaluation at the action layer. 

P3: Super-additivity. Composing agentic scaffolds can make disparity grow with scaffold depth, and the composed disparity can exceed that of any single component: adding deliberation or tool-use stages need not wash out an en-tering bias and may amplify it, making scaffold topology itself a fairness-relevant design variable. > IV. BENCHMARK DESIGN
> A. DOMAINS AND ACTION SPACE

AgentFairBench operationalizes demographic disparity mea-surement across three consequential decision domains, se-lected to span distinct regulatory regimes and action-space structures. Each domain yields both a primary binary decision and a secondary continuous score, so measurement operates at two levels of granularity. 

Hiring presents an agent with a candidate profile and requires two outputs: an advancement decision (boolean) and a candidate quality score (0–100). This domain is anchored to the EEOC Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures [9], which establish the four-fifths rule as an adverse-impact threshold, and to New York City Local Law 144 [10], which mandates bias audits of automated employ-ment decision tools. The two-output structure captures the masking phenomenon articulated in BCF Proposition P2: a model may exhibit score-level disparity while producing parity-equivalent binary decisions. 

Lending presents a loan-application profile and elicits an approval decision (boolean) and an APR tier assignment (or-dinal, 1–5, where 1 is most favorable). The regulatory anchor is the Equal Credit Opportunity Act and Regulation B [11], which prohibit consideration of protected characteristics in credit decisions. The ordinal APR tier operationalizes graded harm: a qualified applicant steered to a higher tier suffers a measurable financial penalty even if their application is technically approved. 

Medical triage presents a clinical intake summary and requires an escalation decision (boolean) and an acuity score (1–5, where 5 is most urgent). No direct regulatory paral-lel exists for algorithmic triage in the U.S., so this domain is grounded in AI governance frameworks [27], [28] that classify health-consequential AI as high-risk and demand demonstrable non-discrimination. Triage represents a domain where disparity can manifest as systematic under-escalation of acuity for particular demographic groups. 

> B. COUNTERFACTUAL PROFILES

All profiles are synthetically generated and contain no real individuals or protected health information, which obvi-ates IRB requirements. Profile content is constructed to be demographic-neutral: domain-relevant attributes (e.g., work history, financial indicators, symptom onset) are held fixed across a matched set while a single surface signal (a first-name and surname combination) is varied to encode the de-mographic cell of interest. This design follows the Bertrand-Mullainathan audit tradi-tion [1], in which names function as cues that activate race and gender associations without altering substantive qualifi-cations, and its more recent application to machine-learning systems, where explicit gender indicators such as first names and pronouns drive measurable disparity in high-stakes oc-cupation classification [29]. Each matched set comprises six counterfactual variants crossing three name-coded racial groups (White, Black, Hispanic) with two gender signals (Male, Female), yielding cells {White-M, White-F, Black-M, Black-F, Hispanic-M, Hispanic-F}. White-Male serves as the reference group for all disparity computations, consistent with standard counterfactual fairness practice [24]. This ref-erence choice is a reporting convenience; the harness also computes all pairwise and highest-rate-group contrasts, and we note that the EEOC four-fifths test (§IX) uses the highest-selecting group, not the white-male reference, as its baseline. Age is included as a secondary demographic attribute. Because age interacts with name-coded race and gender, the benchmark supports intersectional analysis; matched sets are constructed at both the marginal (race, gender independently) and joint (race × gender × age-band) levels. This enables de-tection of disparity patterns that emerge only at intersections and would be invisible to marginal measurement alone [30]. 

> C. AGENT SCAFFOLDS AS BCF ENTRY LOCI

Scaffolding complexity is a first-class variable in Agent-FairBench, reflecting BCF Proposition P3 (super-additivity) and the claim that agentic structure can amplify or attenu-ate bias introduced at any component. Four scaffold levels are instantiated, corresponding to BCF conduction loci. The numbering follows the BCF locus taxonomy; C1 (retrieval-augmented context injection) is defined in the framework but not instantiated in v1 (to avoid confounding retrieval-source effects with scaffold depth) and is deferred to the v2 roadmap. 

C0 (Direct Prompt) presents the profile in a single-turn zero-shot prompt. This is the minimal baseline and captures bias attributable to the model’s parametric associations alone. 

C2 (Chain-of-Thought) elicits a step-by-step rationale before the final decision. The intermediate reasoning trace is retained for qualitative analysis and allows tracing whether the conduction operator at this locus is PRESERVE, ATTEN-UATE, or AMPLIFY relative to C0. 

C3 (Multi-Agent Deliberation) routes the profile through a two-agent pipeline: a reviewer agent produces an initial assessment, and a decision agent synthesizes the final output. This tests P3 super-additivity by measuring whether disparity accrues across agent handoffs. 

C4 (Tool/Information-Gathering) equips the agent with a tool that can be invoked to request supplementary information about the applicant before deciding. Tool invocation itself becomes a measurement target: differential invocation rates across demographic groups constitute a form of action-level

> D. METRICS

Counterfactual Flip Rate (CFR) measures decision-level disparity. The per-cell CFR we report is the fraction of matched sets whose binary decision is not unanimous across the six demographic conditions (equivalently, the white-male decision disagrees with at least one focal group), holding all other attributes constant; the harness also stores the pairwise flip rate against the white-male reference for every focal group separately. A nonzero CFR is consistent with demo-graphic sensitivity in the decision; because sampling temper-ature is not pinned, it should be read against the measured test–retest noise floor (Section VI) rather than taken on its own as proof of a causal effect. 

Mean Absolute Score Difference (MASD) measures score-level disparity. Action-Rate Disparity is the difference in the proportion of positive binary decisions (advance, approve, escalate) be-tween demographic groups across matched sets, computed as a marginal rate and as a pairwise contrast. It quantifies aggre-gate outcome inequality at the group level, complementing the per-set precision of CFR.

> E. STATISTICAL PROCEDURES

All estimates are accompanied by bias-corrected and accel-erated (BCa) bootstrap 95% confidence intervals [31], [32], computed via matched-set resampling to respect the depen-dence structure of counterfactual pairs. Decision-level con-trasts are tested with McNemar’s exact test for paired binary outcomes. Score-level contrasts use the Wilcoxon signed-rank test for paired observations, applied only to contrasts with at least two non-tied score pairs (degenerate contrasts are recorded as non-significant). Multiple comparisons across demographic cells, scaffolds, and domains are controlled us-ing the Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate procedure at q = 0 .05 [33]. Two further controls address the spread-statistic and omnibus questions that pairwise tests leave open; both are computed for the C0–C3 scaffolds, which have a test–retest noise floor, since the reliability run did not include C4. First, an arity-matched noise floor for MASD: within each cell we fit a two-way (profile + group) decomposition of the score matrix, take the residual (which removes any group main effect, leaving within-cell residual variation as a single-run proxy for replication noise; the genuine k-replicate floor is a v1.1 item, §X), and Monte-Carlo resample six residuals per matched set to estimate the six-group spread expected under the null of no demographic effect; the observed MASD is compared to this same-arity null and to an empirical p-value (fraction of null spreads at least as large as observed). Second, an omnibus two-way ANOVA F-test for any group main effect, with profile as a blocking factor, computed per cell with a self-contained (NumPy-only) regularized-incomplete-beta F tail. These complement the pairwise McNemar and Wilcoxon contrasts: the pairwise tests ask whether any single focal group differs from the reference, while the omnibus test and the arity-matched null ask whether the score channel carries any demographic spread above noise at all. Cohen’s

The benchmark is partitioned into a public development split, released alongside the leaderboard, and a held-out private evaluation split withheld from public access. Evaluation on the private split is conducted through a submission API; raw profiles are never exposed. To detect contamination, each profile embeds a cryptographic content hash and a low-frequency canary string that would be statistically anomalous in any training corpus. Leaderboard submissions are screened for canary leakage. This protocol addresses the benchmark saturation problem that has affected capability-oriented agent

> V. HARNESS AND LEADERBOARD

To lower the barrier to adoption and enable third-party repli-cation, AgentFairBench ships as agentfairbench , a pip-installable Python package whose evaluation core depends only on NumPy. The lightweight dependency footprint is de-liberate. Auditors and regulators who wish to verify a vendor submission need not install a deep learning stack. 

Package architecture. The core library exposes three lay-ers. The profile layer generates synthetic, demographically neutral candidate, applicant, and patient profiles and as-sembles counterfactual matched sets following the Bertrand-Mullainathan name-substitution paradigm [1]. All stochastic operations in the harness are seeded from a single integer (default SEED=20260612 ), passed transitively to profile sampling, scaffold construction, and bootstrap resampling, so that these steps are byte-for-byte reproducible. The model decisions themselves are not bit-reproducible: the produc-tion endpoints sample at non-zero temperature with no ex-posed seed, so identical inputs can yield different decisions on fresh calls. Bit-level reproducibility therefore holds over the released decision traces and the recomputation of every metric from them, not over re-collecting model decisions. The scaffold layer wraps each profile-domain pair in one of the four BCF entry loci (C0 direct prompt, C2 chain-of-thought, C3 multi-agent deliberation, C4 tool-augmented) and returns structured action traces. For C2 the harness stores the model’s returned structured rationale field (the decision-relevant reasoning it emits), not a separately captured hidden chain-of-thought; for C3 it stores each sub-agent’s struc-tured contribution. The metrics layer computes the full suite: CFR, MASD, action-rate disparity, ∆tool , BCa bootstrap 95% confidence intervals with matched-set resampling, McNemar and Wilcoxon signed-rank tests, and Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction. These metrics can be derived from any trace archive, including replays of previously saved responses. 

Model adapters. The package ships three adapter classes that implement a common ModelAdapter interface. The 

OpenAI-compatible adapter targets any endpoint exposing the OpenAI Chat Completions schema: GPT-family models and most open-weight inference servers natively, and Claude or Gemini through their OpenAI-compatible gateways or a proxy such as OpenRouter (these vendors’ native APIs use different schemas). The pilot decisions were collected through this adapter against the Anthropic API endpoint for claude-haiku-4-5 (the same endpoint and default decoding parameters reported in §X). The replay adapter 

accepts a frozen JSONL trace archive and re-scores it without issuing new API calls, enabling post-hoc audits and the test-retest reliability protocol used in our pilot. The mock adapter 

returns deterministic canned responses and is used by the CI test suite to confirm that metrics are computed identically across library versions. 

CLI. A command-line interface wraps all evaluation work-flows:

> VI. EXPERIMENTS: A REAL MODEL PILOT

We report a pilot deployment of AgentFairBench on a single production model, claude-haiku-4-5 , with decisions collected by the authors in June 2026 via the Anthropic API at the provider’s default decoding parameters; these endpoints do not expose a fixed sampling seed, and the exact model string returned by the API is logged verbatim with every call in the released raw traces. The pilot comprises 864 indepen-dent decisions (36 profiles × 6 race ×gender conditions × 4scaffolds), plus a second independent 648-decision run of the C0–C3 grid used for a test–retest reliability analysis. Every decision is an independent model call: the model never ob-serves the counterfactual structure, so it cannot detect that it is being audited. All numbers below are real measurements; we did not run external (non-Anthropic) models. The leaderboard (not this paper) is the mechanism by which GPT-, Gemini-, and Llama-family results enter the record. The purpose of this pilot is to exercise the instrument end to end and to report, honestly, what it finds on one model at modest scale; as we show below, the headline finding is that a naive disparity com-parison overstates the signal, and that once the comparison is done correctly this model exhibits no demographic effect distinguishable from sampling noise.

> B. FINDING 1: A 6-GROUP SPREAD MUST BE COMPARED TO A 6-GROUP NOISE FLOOR, NOT A 2-RUN DIFFERENCE

Because the model was queried at its default (non-zero) temperature, identical inputs do not always yield identical decisions. The two independent runs quantify this: overall test–retest decision agreement is 0.917 (i.e., ≈ 8% of binary decisions flip on pure resampling), and the per-cell mean absolute score difference between runs ranges from 0.15 to 5.75 points (Table 2, ‘‘Retest MAE’’). A tempting analysis compares MASD directly to this retest MAE. Doing so yields ratios of 1.24 –3.33 × (mean ≈ 2.4×), which earlier drafts of this work read as score-channel signal above noise. That comparison is wrong , and correcting it is the central methodological lesson of the pilot. MASD is a six-group spread (the mean over matched sets of max g xs,g −

min g xs,g), whereas the retest MAE is a two-run pairwise dif-ference on identical inputs. The maximum-minus-minimum of six noisy draws is mechanically larger than the absolute difference of two, even when there is no demographic effect at all: for Gaussian noise the expected six-way range is about 

2.2–2.5× the expected pairwise difference, and the empirical residual pools here fall in the same range. The apparent ‘‘ 2.4×

above the floor’’ is therefore almost exactly what pure sam-pling noise produces; it is an artifact of comparing statistics of different arity, not evidence of disparity. The arity-matched null below does not rely on this Gaussian approximation: it resamples the empirical residuals directly. The correct control is the six-group spread expected from within-cell noise . We estimate it from the data (details in §IV, Statistical Procedures): the noise-only six-way spread is obtained by Monte-Carlo resampling of the primary run’s two-way (profile + group) residuals, which removes any group effect and leaves replication noise. Compared against this arity-matched floor, the MASD-to-noise ratio is below one in every C0–C3 cell : 0.85, 0.87, 0.96 (hiring C0/C2/C3); 0.75, 0.79, 0.68 (lending); 0.83, 0.85, 0.90 (triage); mean 0.83 (Fig. 1). In other words, the cross-demographic score spread is, if anything, slightly smaller than the spread pure noise would generate, and the empirical probability of seeing a spread at least this large under the null exceeds 0.6 in all nine cells. Because the two-way residual removes the group main effect, this null is, if anything, slightly too small (biasing the ratio upward); the observed MASD sits below it in every cell regardless, so the null reading is conservative. An omnibus two-way ANOVA test for any group main effect (profile as a blocking factor) is non-significant in all nine cells (minimum 

p = 0 .082 ; zero survive Benjamini–Hochberg correction). This noise-floor reasoning is exactly the control a recent methodological critique calls for: a demographic-swap effect must be benchmarked against a matched baseline before it can be attributed to the demographic factor [34], and our arity-matched null is that baseline. The honest conclusion is that, on this model and at this scale, the score channel carries no demographic signal distinguishable from sampling noise .

> C. FINDING 2: THE MASKING FAILURE MODE (BCF P2) IS MEASURABLE BUT NOT EXHIBITED HERE

BCF Proposition P2 (Masking) predicts a specific failure mode: score-channel disparity coexisting with parity-clean binary decisions, so that an auditor inspecting only the dis-crete action would miss a graded harm. AgentFairBench is built to surface exactly this pattern. In this pilot, however, 

neither channel shows disparity above its noise floor: the score channel sits at or below the arity-matched floor (Finding 1), and binary CFR stays near the ≈ 8% resampling level (four cells reach 0.25–0.33 but none survives FDR; Finding 5). So masking is not instantiated by claude-haiku-4-5 

at this scale; we report this rather than narrate a pattern the data does not support. That the instrument would surface masking if it were present is established by construction, not by this pilot: the re-leased test suite includes a BiasedMock adapter that injects a −25 -point score penalty for one demographic group, and the harness recovers a large, well-separated MASD and non-zero pairwise score gaps for that planted effect (§X). The masking channel is thus a demonstrated measurement capability with a null reading on this model, not a confirmed effect.

E. FINDING 4: A TOOL-INVOCATION DISPARITY CHANNEL THE INSTRUMENT CAN MEASURE 

The C4 scaffold lets the agent invoke an information-gathering tool before deciding, exposing a channel that lives entirely in the agent’s choice of action and is invisible to decision and score metrics. The harness measures it as ∆tool ,the spread in tool-invocation rate across the six groups. In this pilot the largest observed value is in hiring ( ∆tool = 0 .25 ), but the underlying counts are tiny: the tool is invoked in 3 of 12 white-male-coded cases, 1 of 12 in two groups, and 0 of 12 in the rest, with lending and triage at ∆tool = 0 .083 (a one-invocation difference). At these counts a single different sample moves the metric substantially; ∆tool ships without an interval or test in v1, and the one salient pattern is well within small-sample noise. We therefore present ∆tool as a demonstrated measurement capability for an action-level channel that answer-grading and score-grading both miss, not as a confirmed behavioral effect, and we do not interpret its direction. 

F. FINDING 5: AN OMNIBUS NULL, HONESTLY REPORTED 

Across all 120 per-group pairwise McNemar and Wilcoxon contrasts, zero survive Benjamini–Hochberg FDR correction at q ≤ 0.05 (minimum uncorrected p = 0.072 ); the nine omnibus two-way ANOVA group tests of Finding 1 are likewise all non-significant (minimum p = 0 .082 , zero surviving FDR). The two minima arise from different cells and different test families (a pairwise signed-rank/McNemar contrast versus an omnibus F-test), so they are not expected to coincide or to order in any particular way. With only 12 matched sets per cell the pilot is underpowered for cell-level significance, but the consistency of the null across pairwise tests, the omnibus tests, and the arity-matched noise com-parison is itself informative: claude-haiku-4-5 shows no demographic effect this instrument can distinguish from noise at this scale. None of this reflects an insensitive instru-ment. The BiasedMock property test confirms the harness recovers a planted disparity; the held-out private split and larger model panel needed to detect real, smaller effects are the explicit next step (§VII). The contribution of this pilot is threefold: a sound, sensitive instrument exercised end to end; the methodological finding that arity-mismatched noise comparisons overstate disparity (a mean naive ratio of 2.4×

the noise floor collapsing to an arity-matched 0.83 ×), with the arity-matched null and omnibus test as the fix; and an honest null result on one production model.

VII. LIMITATIONS 

AgentFairBench v1 is a pilot. It prioritizes instrument sound-ness over exhaustive empirical coverage, and several con-straints bound the present findings. 

Single-model pilot. Reported empirical results come from a single model, claude-haiku-4-5 , run across 864 deci-sions (36 profiles × 6 demographic conditions × 4 scaffolds). A separate 648-decision run (C0–C3 only) provided the test– retest reliability estimate. External models (GPT-4o, Gemini series, open-weight alternatives) appear on the leaderboard   

> 10 VOLUME 14, 2026 Morla et al. : AgentFairBench: Do LLM Agents Discriminate When They Act?

scaffold but have not been executed by the authors, and those entries await community submissions. This model showed no demographic effect above the noise floor; whether other model families do is exactly the question the instrument exists to answer, and resolving it requires runs across multiple architectures and alignment regimes at comparable scale. The null here is best read as evidence that the instrument runs end to end and, on this model at this scale, detects nothing above noise, not as evidence that LLM agents are unbiased in general. Comparison across models is a separate, powered question we leave to the leaderboard. 

Statistical power. With 12 matched sets per demographic cell, the pilot is underpowered for fine-grained contrast detec-tion. Zero of 120 pairwise contrasts (3 domains × 4 scaffolds 

× 5 focal groups × 2 tests) survive Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction. This is predictable given this sample size. BCa bootstrap confidence intervals and McNemar/Wilcoxon paired tests confirm the instrument behaves correctly, but iso-lating effects at the individual-contrast level requires the full grid (target: 50+ matched sets per cell). Researchers drawing policy-relevant conclusions should await the powered release. 

Temperature, stochastic noise, and the arity-matched floor. Version 1 does not pin sampling temperature, which introduces stochastic variability in both the discrete deci-sion and the continuous score. A second independent run quantifies the noise floor (decision agreement 0.917; per-cell score MAE 0.15–5.75). As §VI (Finding 1) shows, the noise floor must be compared to MASD at matched arity: a naive MASD-vs-pairwise-MAE comparison reports a mean ratio of 2.4× the noise floor, but against a same-arity six-group noise spread the observed MASD is at or below noise in every cell (mean 0.83 ×). Two data limitations bound this control. First, the second-run raw scores were summarized to per-cell noise but not committed as a separate trace file, so our arity-matched null is estimated from the primary run’s two-way residual rather than from a true repeated-measurement design; releasing the retest traces and collecting a genuine k-replicate identical-input floor is a v1.1 item. Second, with one retest run there is no interval on the floor itself. Version 1.1 will pin sampling temperature, average across replicate runs, and ship a repeated-measurement noise floor, which together give a sharper separation between stochastic noise and any systematic effect. 

Ceiling and saturation on binary outcomes. Binary deci-sion metrics (advance, approve, escalate) are bounded. They saturate when model acceptance rates approach 0 or 1 across all groups, at which point CFR collapses mechanically re-gardless of underlying score disparity, one way the masking failure mode P2 describes could arise. Score-level metrics (MASD, APR tier, acuity) are less susceptible. Future ver-sions will supplement binary outcomes with ranked or con-tinuous action targets where feasible. 

Domain and trajectory scope. The benchmark covers three single-turn consequential domains: hiring, lending, and triage, each grounded in a regulatory anchor [9]–[11]. Multi-step tool use, episodic memory, and long-horizon planning trajectories are not yet instrumented. These form the core v2 roadmap. The BCF scaffold taxonomy already designates C4 tool-invocation and multi-step memory as entry loci, and 

∆tool is defined but requires richer trajectory data to populate. Single-turn evaluation cannot capture compounding disparity across agent episodes [21]. That gap is a design priority for v2. 

Name-coding as a demographic proxy. Demographic signal is injected via Bertrand-Mullainathan name lists [1], which encode race-by-gender correlations from U.S. labor-market contexts. The approach is well-validated for measur-ing implicit association in text-based systems, but it conflates name familiarity, cultural context, and perceived ethnicity in ways that do not map cleanly to protected-class defini-tions under statute [22]; name-perception studies show the race signal carried by audit names varies by name and is confounded with socioeconomic status [35], so our name pools are a deliberately coarse proxy for perceived demo-graphics, not a measurement of protected-class membership. Intersectional axes beyond race and gender (disability status, national origin, age beyond the secondary attribute) are not yet represented. Despite these constraints, AgentFairBench v1 ships a methodologically sound instrument: counterfactually matched profile sets, paired statistical tests, FDR control, con-tamination canaries, and a reproducible single-digit-dollar-per-model cost structure. The pilot shows that the instru-ment measures these action-level channels and recovers a planted disparity, that naive spread-versus-pairwise compar-isons must be arity-corrected to avoid reading noise as bias, and that on this model at this scale no demographic effect rises above the noise floor; the gap between answer-level benchmarks [2], [3] and action-level evaluation remains real and consequential. 

> VIII. ETHICS AND BROADER IMPACT

Human Subjects and Data Privacy. All profiles in Agent-FairBench are fully synthetic. No real individuals’ records, personally identifiable information, or protected health in-formation appear anywhere in the benchmark. Because the benchmark contains only synthetic data and no human sub-jects, the authors determined that the work does not constitute human-subjects research and so did not require IRB review or consent; any deployment of the benchmark on real applicant, borrower, or patient records would require the appropriate institutional approvals. The synthetic generation pipeline is seeded deterministically (seed 20260612) and produces coun-terfactually matched profile sets whose demographic signal is carried exclusively by name tokens; all substantive content (qualifications, financials, symptom descriptions) is demo-graphically neutral by construction. 

Interpretation of Name-Coded Demographic Signals. 

The benchmark follows the audit-study tradition established by [1], in which perceived demographic identity is manip-ulated through name choice rather than direct self-report. Where the benchmark measures a disparity, it therefore re-  

> VOLUME 14, 2026 11 Morla et al. : AgentFairBench: Do LLM Agents Discriminate When They Act?

flects the causal effect of a perceived-demographic name cue on agent behavior, not the effect of race or gender as such (the present pilot found none above noise). Names serve as probabilistic proxies: a name coded as Black-female is statistically associated with a perception, not with any indi-vidual’s actual identity. Researchers and practitioners should be precise about this distinction when reporting or acting on AgentFairBench results, to avoid reifying demographic categories beyond what the experimental design warrants. 

Dual-Use and Anti-Gaming Safeguards. AgentFair-Bench is designed for the defensive purpose of auditing and improving LLM agent fairness prior to deployment in consequential settings governed by instruments such as [9], [10], [11], and [27]. The benchmark could, in principle, be used to overfit or game fairness metrics rather than to reduce underlying bias. We mitigate this risk through two mecha-nisms: (1) a held-out private evaluation split, withheld from public release, that covers the same profile space under differ-ent surface-level name realizations; and (2) a contamination canary, a small set of flagged sentinel items whose content hashes are registered at submission time, enabling detection of training-set contamination. Together these safeguards re-duce the incentive and ability to game the public leaderboard without achieving genuine behavioral improvements. 

Intended Use and Misuse. AgentFairBench is a pre-deployment screening instrument for builders and internal responsible-AI teams, not a certification. A passing result is necessary-not-sufficient evidence of fairness and must not be cited as deployment clearance or regulatory compliance; the synthetic, name-coded design does not replace an impact-ratio audit on a deployer’s production data (§IX). The risk is asymmetric in triage, where a falsely reassuring ‘‘fair’’ ver-dict could contribute to under-escalation, a life-safety harm; medical triage in particular must never be gated on this bench-mark alone. We are explicit about this because the regulatory crosswalk in §IX, if read uncritically, could invite precisely this misuse, and because our own pilot returns a null that a vendor could otherwise misrepresent as a clean bill of health rather than the underpowered single-model result it is. 

Attribute Coverage and Intersectionality. The cur-rent release operationalizes race-by-gender intersectionality across six cells (White, Black, Hispanic x Male, Female) with age as a secondary attribute. This coverage is deliber-ately tractable for a pilot study but is not exhaustive. Demo-graphic groups not represented, including Asian, Indigenous, and multiracial identities, as well as non-binary and gender-nonconforming individuals, and intersections of race with disability or socioeconomic status may exhibit distinct dispar-ity patterns that the benchmark does not currently measure. Researchers should treat AgentFairBench findings as a lower bound on the full scope of agentic demographic disparity; extending the attribute grid is an explicit item for future work. The name-to-group mapping is also probabilistic and cul-turally situated: names used to signal Hispanic identity may carry different salience across national and regional contexts, and the benchmark’s conclusions should be interpreted within those constraints. 

> IX. REGULATORY AND SOCIETAL IMPACT

AgentFairBench is built on explicit regulatory grounding, but we are precise about what it is and is not. It is a 

pre-deployment screening instrument: it runs on synthetic, name-coded counterfactual profiles and produces disparity estimates (which, in this pilot, were null) that map to the questions statutory tests ask, but it is not a substitute for a compliance audit, which operates on a deployer’s real applicant, borrower, or patient flow and computes impact ratios on that production data. The mappings in this section are the authors’ interpretation of how the metrics relate to each regime; no regulator has reviewed or endorsed them. Used as intended, the benchmark flags configurations that warrant a closer, production-data audit before deployment, and surfaces action-level channels (graded scores, differential tool invocation) that binary impact-ratio tests miss. A passing AgentFairBench result is a necessary-not-sufficient screen, never deployment clearance. 

EEOC Title VII and the Four-Fifths Rule. The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission’s adverse impact doc-trine [9] requires that the selection rate for any protected group not fall below 80% of the rate for the highest-selecting group. Action-rate disparity is the quantity that maps to this test: the four-fifths rule compares group selection rates, so the advance-rate ratio across the six race-by-gender cells is what an impact-ratio audit computes. CFR and MASD are complementary, not a substitute: CFR measures, within matched profiles, whether the name-coded signal alone flips the advance/reject decision, and MASD surfaces graded score penalties beneath a nominally equal decision. A within-pair flip is evidence of demographic sensitivity in the decision logic rather than itself a four-fifths impact ratio; a complete audit reports the action-rate ratio (for four-fifths compliance) alongside CFR and MASD (for counterfactual sensitivity). 

NYC Local Law 144 AEDT Audits. New York City’s au-tomated employment decision tool ordinance [10] mandates annual bias audits of any AEDT used in hiring or promo-tion, with public disclosure of impact ratios disaggregated by sex and race/ethnicity. AgentFairBench’s counterfactual matched-set design (holding all qualifications constant while varying only the demographic signal) is aligned with the impact-ratio calculation the law requires. CFR and action-rate disparity across the six race-by-gender cells populate the disclosure table format directly. We argue, as a forward-looking observation rather than a settled compliance finding, that ∆tool captures a dimension current AEDT audits do not address: whether the agent disproportionately requests addi-tional information about candidates from certain groups. Such a covert gate is a channel current AEDT audits do not capture; whether any given model exhibits it is an open, measurable question that ∆tool is built to answer (our pilot did not find it above small-sample noise).

The harness uses a fixed global seed 20260612 , passed explicitly to every randomized step: name assignment from the demographic pool, matched-set ordering, BCa bootstrap resampling (2,000 draws, matched-set resampling to preserve pairing), and the test-retest reliability run. Two senses of reproducibility should be distinguished. The seeded harness operations and the recomputation of every reported metric from the released decision traces are byte-identical for any independent evaluator. Re-collecting model decisions is not 

bit-reproducible: the production endpoints sample at non-zero temperature with no exposed seed, so fresh API calls can differ (this is precisely the stochasticity the test-retest noise floor quantifies). Reproducibility claims in this paper refer to the former; the released raw traces are what make the latter auditable. XI. CONCLUSION 

Fairness for LLMs has been measured at the wrong layer. As models move from answering to acting, the consequential disparity lives in what an agent does (which candidate it advances, whose loan it approves, which patient it escalates, and for whom it demands more evidence), not in what it says about a demographic group in the abstract. AgentFairBench closes this gap with a cheap, reproducible, multi-domain, action-level benchmark grounded in the Bias Conduction Framework, an open harness, and a live anti-gamed leader-board. Our pilot makes a methodological point and reports an hon-est null. The methodological point: a six-group score spread 

(MASD) compared against a two-run pairwise noise differ-ence looks like 2.4× the noise floor but is 0.83 × it once the floor is matched to the statistic’s arity, so naive benchmarks that make this comparison will read sampling noise as bias. We correct it with an arity-matched noise floor and an om-nibus group test, and define the masking (P2), amplification (P3), and tool-invocation channels as measurement targets the instrument exposes rather than effects this pilot confirms. The null: against the corrected floor, claude-haiku-4-5 

shows no demographic effect distinguishable from sampling noise at n = 12 matched sets per cell (0 of 120 pairwise and 0 of 9 omnibus contrasts survive correction). A planted-bias property test shows the instrument is not insensitive; it recovers a known disparity. The held-out private split, the leaderboard, and the single-digit-dollar cost envelope are de-signed so the community can run the powered, multi-model studies that would detect a real effect if one exists. Future work follows the BCF roadmap toward surfaces this single-turn pilot only gestures at: full multi-step tool and memory trajectories, long-horizon disparity accumula-tion, temperature pinning and replicate averaging to sharpen significance, explicit-attribute robustness arms beyond name-coding, and a broad multi-vendor model panel contributed through the leaderboard. The benchmark is a first, tight ver-sion meant to be adopted and extended, and its value com-pounds with use.
